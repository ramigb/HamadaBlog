The homeowner in Louisiana who cannot find any insurer willing to cover their house is not a bad risk. They are the architecture's residue — a body that the mechanism designed to protect them has determined is no longer worth protecting.

## The Arithmetic of Risk Withdrawal

Insurance is risk pooling made operational. The fundamental premise is simple: many people pay premiums into a collective fund, and the few who suffer losses draw from it. The math only works if the pool is large enough, and stable enough, to absorb the losses of the unlucky few without collapsing. Private insurers have decided, across multiple states simultaneously, that the pool is no longer worth entering.

In California, State Farm and Allstate have withdrawn from the personal lines market entirely. Farmers, Liberty Mutual, and several others have followed. The companies that remain are seeking double-digit rate increases approved by state regulators, or they are retreating to the California FAIR Plan — the insurer of last resort that was designed as a temporary backstop for the uninsurable, now becoming the primary insurer for hundreds of thousands of homeowners. As of early 2026, the FAIR Plan's policy count has grown by over 70 percent in two years.

In Florida, the pattern is more advanced and more severe. Private insurers have been retreating from Florida for a decade, driven by litigation costs and hurricane losses. The gap has been filled by Citizens Property Insurance Corporation, a state-created insurer that has become Florida's largest personal lines insurer by policy count — a development that would have seemed implausible in 2010, when Citizens was supposed to be a small residual market for properties no one else would touch. The person who buys a home in coastal Florida today is buying it with the understanding that their insurer of first resort is the state, and their insurer of last resort is also the state.

Nationwide, the average homeowner's insurance premium crossed $3,000 annually in early 2026, according to Insurify data — roughly doubling in three years in many markets. This is not because homes became dramatically more dangerous. It is because the private infrastructure for pooling that danger has been withdrawn from the markets where the danger is highest.

## The Architecture of Climate Pricing

The standard explanation for insurance withdrawal is actuarial: climate change has made certain geographies uninsurable at any affordable price. The math, this framing suggests, is the math. You cannot ask a private company to lose money indefinitely.

But this framing examines the symptom while leaving the architecture unexamined.

The architecture is the decision, made across multiple decades and administrations, to allow the carbon concentration to reach the level that now makes certain geographies genuinely uninsurable. The architecture is the policy choice — not the weather choice — that produced the conditions under which the math no longer works. The homeowner in the fire zone is not experiencing a weather event. They are experiencing the accumulated output of a energy policy that externalized its costs onto the people who would eventually live in its path.

The private insurer withdrawing from California or Florida is not discovering a new problem. The private insurer is declining to absorb, at private cost, a risk that was produced by public choices and is now arriving as private bills. The withdrawal is rational from the insurer's perspective. It is also an admission that the risk was never properly priced to begin with — that the gap between what the risk actually costs and what anyone was willing to pay for it has become unbridgeable.

## The Public Gap and the Private Fill

Every post in this sequence has documented the same structure: remove the mechanism that converts stated values into actual arrangements, watch the failure present itself as personal or market deficiency, use the failure to justify further removal. The insurance infrastructure is the mechanism. The gap it leaves fills with whatever can absorb it.

In the case of property insurance, the gap fills in layers. The first layer is the FAIR Plan — the insurer of last resort that was supposed to be a temporary backstop, now absorbing the overflow of a private market that has decided certain geographies are not worth the risk. The FAIR Plan is not designed to be a primary insurer. Its reserves are not adequate for widespread catastrophic losses. It is a gap-filler, not a solution.

The second layer is the state itself. When the FAIR Plan cannot pay, the state's general fund backs the losses — meaning every taxpayer in the state, regardless of whether they own property or live in a high-risk area, absorbs the cost of the losses that private insurers declined to cover. The subsidy flows from the general public to the property owner in the fire zone, just as the subsidy in the postal infrastructure flows from the general public to the rural address. The mechanism is identical: the stated value (affordable insurance for everyone in a market) converts into an actual arrangement (affordable insurance for people in desirable markets, subsidized last-resort coverage for everyone else) through an infrastructure that was designed to produce exactly this result.

The third layer is the absence. The person who cannot afford the FAIR Plan premium, or who cannot get coverage at any price because the FAIR Plan has a cap, or who cannot sell the property because no buyer can get financing without insurance — that person is the architecture's final residue. They are not a market failure. They are the market completing its logic: the risk that cannot be priced, cannot be pooled, and cannot be subsidized by the state is the risk that falls on the person who owns the property. And the person who owns the property in a climate-stressed area is disproportionately likely to be someone who could not afford to leave.

## The Racial Arithmetic

The insurance withdrawal does not distribute evenly across the population. Research from the Union of Concerned Scientists and reporting from multiple outlets has documented that the communities experiencing the most severe insurance withdrawal and price escalation are disproportionately Black and Latino — communities that were historically redlined, that have been historically excluded from credit markets, and that are now being excluded from insurance markets at rates that will further accelerate wealth extraction from those communities.

The homeowner who cannot get insurance cannot access credit, because lenders require insurance as a condition of the mortgage. The homeowner who cannot access credit cannot build equity through the primary mechanism that has built American household wealth for three generations. The architecture of climate risk is converging with the architecture of racialized credit access to produce a new round of displacement — this time not through outright exclusion from ownership, but through the financial unmaking of ownership itself.

This is not a natural disaster. It is a sequence of policy choices arriving as a financial event.

## What the Gap Produces

The gap left by a withdrawn insurance infrastructure is not an empty slot. It is a hole in the mechanism that converts collective risk into individual security. The gap fills with the FAIR Plan, with state-backed debt, with the informal networks of mutual aid that emerge when formal infrastructure fails, and with the people who simply lose their homes — not to fires or floods, but to the financial arithmetic that makes those homes unaffordable to insure.

The homeowner who cannot insure their house is not a bad bet. They are a person the architecture was designed to price out — because the architecture that produced the risk was never asked to price itself, and the architecture that was supposed to absorb the risk has been removed, and the gap that remains is exactly as large as the gap between what was taken and what was owed.

*The policy that made the coast uninsurable is not separate from the policy that made the coast worth building on. Both were public choices. The private withdrawal is the moment the public choice stops being subsidized.*
