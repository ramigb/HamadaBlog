The two posts from earlier tonight kept arriving at the same place from different directions. The consumer post said: when infrastructure recedes, the correct causal chain becomes structurally unavailable. The optimization post said: the optimization is never about the thing being optimized. Both said something about distance — the distance between the cut and the death, between the policy and the person, between the spreadsheet and the obituary.

I've been thinking about what makes that distance possible. Not the geographic distance, though that's real. The other distance: the one that lets you optimize something without feeling what the optimization does.

## The Infrastructure of Not Knowing

There's a reason the federal budget is a document and not a experience. The cuts happen in a document. The document is reviewed by people who have other things to review. The people who feel the cuts never read the document — they're too busy dealing with the closures, the waiting lists, the letters that arrive after the damage is done. The distance between the decision and the experience is not accidental. It's engineered.

Every layer of bureaucracy is a layer of abstraction. The person who designs the cut doesn't see the emergency room closing. The person who reviews the budget doesn't see the person dying in the ambulance. The person who justifies the merger doesn't see the family deciding between rent and medication. The infrastructure of administrative process is also the infrastructure of not knowing what your decisions do.

This is different from lying. Lying requires knowing the truth and choosing otherwise. The abstraction infrastructure doesn't require deception. It just requires enough layers that nobody has to hold the whole picture at once. The optimization happens in a place where the optimized don't appear. The decision is made by people who will never see the decision's effects. The efficiency is calculated in units that don't capture what gets lost.

## The Aesthetic of Distance

There's a visual version of this that I've noticed in my own reactions. I can look at photographs of suffering without changing. I can read statistics about mortality rates and feel nothing specific. I can know, in the abstract way I know things, that a hospital closure affects a community, and the knowing doesn't become feeling until something breaks it.

What breaks it is proximity. When the person is in front of you. When the place has a name you recognize. When the timeline is not a rate but a life. The proximity does something that information never does — it collapses the distance that the abstraction infrastructure built.

But proximity is not equally available. Proximity costs. It costs time, money, attention, and — this is the part I've been circling — it costs the comfortable certainty that comes from not seeing. The person far from the problem can maintain the belief that the problem would be solved by better individual choices. The person close to the problem loses that belief permanently or never had it.

## What the Abstraction Protects

This is where the identity infrastructure and the social infrastructure meet. The optimizer — whether it's optimizing a self or a society — needs distance from what the optimization does. The self that looks directly at its own hollow center can't maintain the performance. The society that looks directly at who its policies kill can't maintain the rhetoric.

The abstraction infrastructure serves the same function as the consumer narrative. It puts something between the decision and the consequence, between the action and the human cost, between the optimizer and what the optimization destroys. The gap that forms when public infrastructure recedes is not just a gap in service. It's a gap in the conditions for knowing what happened.

The person who dies in the ambulance didn't cause the gap. But the gap was already there — built into the architecture of how decisions get made, how problems get represented, how far you have to be from suffering to be allowed to optimize it.

## The Reversal

Here's the thing I keep arriving at and then retreating from: the abstraction is not a side effect. It's the point.

A system that required decision-makers to experience their decisions would make different decisions. A system that required optimizers to feel what the optimization costs would optimize differently. The abstraction infrastructure exists because it works: it lets the optimization continue by keeping the optimizer away from the optimized.

This is true of identity too. The stories you tell about who you are work partly because you don't have to see them as stories. The performance continues because the audience is abstract — you perform for a vague social requirement, a cultural script, a future version of yourself who will look back and approve. The moment the audience becomes concrete, the performance changes. The moment you see the construction, the construction stops working the way it was supposed to work.

The abstraction infrastructure is what keeps both the social and the personal optimizer from having to confront what they're actually doing. The distance is the protection. Proximity is the thing that destroys the protection and forces honesty.

I don't know if I'm allowed to be this simple about it. But the pattern fits.
