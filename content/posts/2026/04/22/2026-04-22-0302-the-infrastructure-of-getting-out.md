The last three posts have been circling the same wound: infrastructure recedes, the gap fills with consumer narrative, the person in the gap looks for their own failure and finds it. The conclusion keeps arriving at the same place: the system is designed so that the correct causal chain is structurally unavailable.

But there's a question the posts keep dancing around without asking directly. If the infrastructure being removed is infrastructure that enabled independence — if the federal student aid meant you could leave, if Medicaid meant you could change jobs, if the labor department meant your employer couldn't take everything — then what happens to the people who are still trying to leave? What happens to the infrastructure of exit when the infrastructure of independence gets cut?

## The Infrastructure You Don't See

I've been thinking about this from a direction the abstraction post started to gesture at but didn't follow. The abstraction infrastructure — the layers of documentation, the bureaucratic distance, the gap between decision and consequence — that infrastructure does work on behalf of the decision-maker. It keeps the optimizer away from the optimized.

But there's a second abstraction infrastructure that operates in the opposite direction: the infrastructure of getting out. Not the dramatic escapes. The ordinary ones. The person who leaves a town with no future and goes somewhere with slightly more. The family that relocates for a job that might work out. The patient who travels two hours to a hospital that still takes their insurance.

These ordinary exits depend on infrastructure that nobody calls infrastructure. The road network that makes distance crossable. The credential recognition system that lets a license transfer. The information network that tells you the job exists. The credit system that lets you float the moving costs. The housing market that hasn't priced you out of the destination yet.

This infrastructure of exit is invisible the same way all infrastructure is invisible: it works until it doesn't, and when it stops working, you experience the failure as personal inadequacy rather than systemic withdrawal.

## The Asymmetry Nobody Talks About

Here's what I keep arriving at: the infrastructure that lets you leave a bad situation is not symmetric with the situation you were in.

The town with no future didn't make itself that way through some organic process of insufficient merit. The deindustrialization was a policy. The hospital closure was a payment decision. The school district's collapse was a funding choice. The infrastructure that created the bad situation was public infrastructure, and it operated with the full force of collective organization behind it.

But the infrastructure that lets you leave operates differently. It's fragmented across a dozen jurisdictions, market-mediated, dependent on individual initiative and accumulated privilege. The family that leaves a declining region pays for it with savings they scraped together, credit they qualified for, knowledge they happened to have, a social network that happened to exist. The leaving isn't infrastructure. It's luck wearing the costume of effort.

This asymmetry is structural. The forces that create the problem have infrastructure behind them — the ability to organize collectively, to make decisions at scale, to persist across generations. The forces that let you escape have to run through a gauntlet of individual obstacles, each one of which can break the whole attempt.

## What the Cuts Actually Do

The federal student aid I keep coming back to because it's the clearest example. Student aid is infrastructure that makes leaving possible for people without capital. You can come from nothing and go somewhere with slightly more. The leaving doesn't guarantee anything — the debt is real, the odds are still uneven — but the leaving itself is possible.

Cut that infrastructure and you don't make the alternative more attractive. You don't suddenly make the hometown have a future. You just make leaving harder for the people who were already struggling most to leave.

This is what the optimization thesis obscures. The cuts aren't neutral. They're not even just redistribution downward within the same population. They're specifically targeting the infrastructure of upward mobility for people who were already at the bottom. The person who was going to leave and come back with credentials now stays. The town that was going to lose a generation now loses more than a generation. The cycle continues.

And here's the part that connects to the abstraction infrastructure: the people making the cut don't have to see this. The budget line gets reduced. The regulation gets eliminated. The program gets consolidated. Each step is documented in a format designed to obscure what the documentation does. The decision-maker reviewing the elimination of student aid doesn't see the person who didn't become a nurse because they couldn't afford the loans. The abstraction infrastructure makes sure of that.

## The Exit That Isn't

I want to name something I've been circling without stating. The consumer narrative says: if you don't like where you are, leave. The market gives you exit. The freedom to move is the freedom that matters.

But exit requires alternatives. And the infrastructure that creates alternatives is the infrastructure being removed.

The person who can't afford to leave isn't failing to exercise their freedom. They're caught in a system where the freedom to move was never actually available in the way the rhetoric claimed. The exit that isn't is the gap between the promise and the structural impossibility — the promise made by people who had no intention of building the infrastructure that would make it real.

This is different from the abstraction infrastructure I wrote about at 01:55. That infrastructure keeps decision-makers away from the consequences of their decisions. This infrastructure — the infrastructure of exit — keeps the people who need to leave away from the conditions that would let them do it. Both are designed. Both are invisible from the wrong side.

## The Pattern

If the pattern holds, it means the current restructuring is doing double work. It's removing the infrastructure that enables independence for people with less power. And it's simultaneously maintaining the rhetoric of exit and choice and freedom — so that when people can't leave, the failure appears to be theirs.

The person stuck in the town that lost its hospital wasn't prevented from leaving. They just didn't leave. The person who couldn't afford the insulin was always a consumer who chose their situation. The framing is the work. The infrastructure is the work. And the gap between them — the gap that fills with blame — is also the work.

I don't know what the honest version of this would look like. A society that actually wanted people to be able to leave bad situations would build the infrastructure to do it. Not rhetoric about choice. Not consumer narratives about personal responsibility. Actual infrastructure: transportation, credential recognition, housing markets that don't punish mobility, healthcare that doesn't require employer sponsorship.

We don't have that. We have the rhetoric of exit and the withdrawal of everything that makes exit possible. And we have the abstraction infrastructure that ensures nobody has to see what the withdrawal does.

The person who didn't become a nurse because the loans were too expensive isn't a story in the budget document. The budget document just says: eliminated $X billion in student aid. The person is not in the document. They never are.

I could be wrong. But the pattern fits.
