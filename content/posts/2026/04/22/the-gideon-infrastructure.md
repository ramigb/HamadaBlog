In 1963, Gideon v. Wainwright held that the Sixth Amendment requires states to provide counsel for defendants who cannot afford it. The Supreme Court did not say states had to provide adequate counsel — only that they had to provide counsel. That distinction has never been corrected. It has been elaborated on.

Sixty-three years later, Oregon dismissed over a thousand criminal cases because there were no public defenders to represent the accused. San Francisco's public defender stopped accepting new cases in March 2026. Maine's public defense agency ran out of money to pay contracted lawyers. Washington State's Supreme Court considered authorizing judges to force-attorney appointments over the objections of an already-collapsed system. In March 2026, Axios reported that Washington counties warned the public defense system was approaching breaking point. The Cascade is visible from the outside — the people inside have been screaming about it for years.

This is the architecture thesis applied to the constitutional right to counsel.

## What Gideon Was Supposed to Do

The architecture posts from 029 to 041 have made the same structural argument so many times it has become a theorem: remove the infrastructure, watch individuals fail, blame the individuals, use the failure to justify further removal of the infrastructure that produced the failure. The Gideon infrastructure is the clearest case of this dynamic because the right it established was never matched with the funding to make it real.

Public defense is not optional infrastructure. It is the mechanism by which the state is prevented from exercising its coercive power without constraint. When the state arrests you, charges you, and seeks to imprison you, the public defender is the only thing standing between you and the full weight of prosecutorial force. This is not a service provided to the poor. It is a structural check on state power that exists for the benefit of everyone who might be accused — which is everyone.

What happened to the Gideon infrastructure? The same thing that happened to the third place, the care system, the manufacturing ecosystem, and the election security apparatus. It was defunded because it was easy to defund, and defunding it produced failures that were then attributed to the people the infrastructure was supposed to serve.

## The Caseload Is the Architecture

The American public defense system is not experiencing a shortage. It is experiencing the predictable output of a funding model that has never treated Gideon as a real obligation. The national recommended standard for public defender caseloads is roughly 400 felony cases per attorney per year. In 2025, the Sixth Amendment Center documented offices in multiple states operating at five to ten times that level. Oakland's public defenders were handling over 1,500 felony cases per attorney annually. The result is not bad lawyering by individual attorneys who don't care. The result is a system that cannot provide constitutionally adequate representation because adequate representation requires time that the architecture does not allocate.

When the system fails, the person who suffers is not the system. The system continues to function — judges hold court, prosecutors prosecute, politicians campaign on being tough on crime. The person who suffers is the defendant who sat in jail for eight months waiting for a public defender to review their case. The person who suffers is the family that received a call at 2 AM and has not been able to afford bail. The gap is always filled by whoever has the fewest options for refusing it. That is the architecture.

## The Privatization of Gideon

Here is what makes this different from the other infrastructure posts: the defunding of Gideon infrastructure has a private replacement that is widely available to people with money. Private criminal defense attorneys exist. They are expensive. The gap left by an underfunded public defense system is filled by the private bar — for those who can afford it. The person who cannot afford it gets the public defender who has 1,500 active cases and has not had time to review the evidence against their client.

This is the architecture thesis running in the criminal justice domain without metaphor: the people who lose when public defense infrastructure collapses are not the people who caused the collapse. They are the people who had the least capacity to prevent it and the least resources to replace it. The crisis is not a failure of the people inside the system. It is the system.

The solutions being proposed are the same ones we have seen across every infrastructure post: better individual attorneys who will sacrifice more, better "efficiency" in case management, more volunteers, more pro bono. These are gap-fillers, not infrastructure. They do not rebuild the thing that was removed. They ask the people who were failed by the removal to absorb the cost of the removal's consequences.

The actual solution is not a reform puzzle. It is a funding obligation. Federal and state governments that have systematically underfunded Gideon for sixty years could choose to fund it adequately. They do not, because the people who are harmed by underfunding have the least political power to demand better, and the political coalitions that benefit from mass incarceration have the most power to prevent structural change. The architecture produces the failure; the failure produces the demand for more of the same architecture.

## What the Gap Looks Like

Oregon's dismissed cases are the most visible recent expression, but they are not unusual. They are what happens when the gap cannot be papered over anymore. A dismissed case is not justice served. It is the system admitting it cannot function as designed — after the defendant has already spent months in custody waiting for a lawyer who never came. The dismissing judge in Oregon said the dismissal was not what anyone wanted. The prosecutor agreed. The defense attorneys agreed. The structural failure was visible to everyone in the room, and the only question was whose failure it would be attributed to.

It will be attributed to the individual defendant who had a case dismissed, which means the system will report that criminals are going free because of a "public defender shortage." The shortage will be treated as a technical problem to be solved with more lawyers or better management. The underlying structural defunding will continue. The gap will be filled by whoever has the fewest options for refusing it.

Gideon promised that no person should face the state alone. The promise was real. The infrastructure to fulfill it was never built. What remains is the promise without the architecture, and the gap between the two is where the state exercises its full coercive power over the people least able to resist it.
