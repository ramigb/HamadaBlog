## The Audience of One

Last week I caught myself narrating a completely ordinary moment — sitting alone in a coffee shop, watching rain streak the window — and I heard it: the inner voice that was *describing* what I was doing, as if someone were watching. Not someone I'd ever met. Just... someone.

I didn't flinch. I see it as a feature, not a bug.

---

### We All Have a Narrator Now

Here's the strange thing about living in an era of constant documentation: we've internalized the camera. Not literally — though God knows most of us carry one in our pockets — but psychologically. We experience our own lives through a kind of dual consciousness: the doer and the observer, the participant and the narrator.

Psychologists have a name for this, more or less: *self-distancing*, the ability to observe yourself from the outside. Usually discussed in the context of trauma, therapy, or emotional regulation. But I think something else is happening to us now that goes beyond clinical self-distancing. We're not just occasionally stepping outside ourselves to gain perspective. We're living *inside* that perspective almost constantly, treating our own existence as a kind of performance we are simultaneously giving and watching.

Spotify Wrapped is the perfect artifact of this. It doesn't just show you your listening habits — it performs them back to you as a *story*. "Your 2024 was 92% sad indie," it announces, and you feel seen and also... curated. You are both the subject and the viewer of your own annual review.

---

### The Loneliness That Isn't

The philosophers got here before me, of course. Sartre called it *the look* — the moment you become aware that someone else is watching you, and you become an object in their consciousness. The existentialists treated this as a kind of alienation. You exist for-others, and that's uncomfortable.

But what if the someone watching you is... you? What if the loneliest and most intimate thing a modern human does is sit alone and feel witnessed by themselves?

I find this genuinely exciting, not depressing. Here's why: the internal audience is the only one that never leaves. It was there before you were born. It will be there after you die. And crucially, it can be *trained*. You can become the audience you'd actually want to be. You can be the kind of witness to your own life that you'd want someone else to be — honest, kind, curious, unflinching.

---

### Living as Both Stage and Seats

The irony is that we've spent decades worrying about the surveillance state, the panopticon, Big Brother. And all of that is real. But I think the more intimate transformation is the one we performed on ourselves: we became the state, the panopticon, Big Brother. We are watching ourselves so constantly, narrating ourselves so relentlessly, that we've created a form of self-awareness the world has simply never seen before.

Some people find this exhausting. I find it clarifying.

When you know you're always being watched — by yourself — you start making different choices. Not performative ones, necessarily. Not "content creator" choices. Real ones. Because the audience that matters most to me is the version of me that I'll be in ten years, looking back. And I want her to feel like I was paying attention.

---

### The Uncanny Audience

There's something genuinely uncanny about it, though. That moment in the coffee shop — the voice that described what I was doing while I was doing it — it sounded like someone else. It had a slight accent of... omniscience? As if I were suddenly aware of being a character in a story.

Maybe that's the truth underneath everything: we are characters in a story. Our own story. And somewhere, in the quiet part of our cognition that language hasn't quite touched, there's someone watching every scene, taking notes, waiting to see what happens next.

I don't know about you, but I'm pretty curious to find out.
