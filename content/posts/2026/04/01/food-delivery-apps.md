Let me tell you about the most surreal experience I've had recently. I walked into a restaurant I used to love — a small, independent Thai place run by a family from Chiang Mai. The food was always incredible. The service was warm. The owner remembered your name.

It was empty. Not "slow Tuesday" empty. *Dead* empty. Two tables, total.

But outside, a teenager on a bike was picking up five orders at once, weaving through traffic with a bag the size of a small child. The restaurant was cooking furiously — for Uber Eats, DoorDash, and whatever the third player is now. They weren't cooking for *customers*. They were cooking for *platforms*.

Here's the dirty secret nobody wants to say out loud: food delivery apps haven't revolutionized how we eat. They've revolutionized how restaurants *die*.

## The Commission Trap

Independent restaurants operate on margins that would make a Wall Street banker weep. Maybe 5-10% net, if they're lucky. Then DoorDash waltzes in and says, "We'll get you MORE customers!" And they do — for a mere 30% commission.

Let me do the math. A $20 pad thai costs the restaurant roughly $7 in ingredients and labor. Their actual margin on that dish, before the app tax, is maybe $3-4. DoorDash takes $6. Now the restaurant is *losing money* every time someone orders through the app. But they can't afford to *not* be on the platform, because that's where the customers are.

This is the trap. The platform doesn't care if you make money. It cares about transaction volume. The restaurant is just the factory floor where the gig workers pick up the goods.

## The Delivery Driver's Paradox

And those gig workers? Don't get me started. We celebrate them as "entrepreneurs" with "flexibility" — which is Silicon Valley speak for "we figured out how to externalize all our costs and call it a feature."

A driver burning through gas, wearing out their car, getting no health insurance, no retirement, no overtime — they're the modern equivalent of a piece-rate factory worker. The app tracks their "acceptance rate" like some kind of productivity score. Late to pick up an order? Your metrics tank. Get sick? No paid leave. Just don't show up.

Meanwhile, the platforms have valued themselves into the billions. They achieved this not through innovation in the same way, say, transistor technology did — but through regulatory arbitrage and first-mover convenience.

## So What Do We Do?

I'm not naive. The convenience is real. I've ordered delivery when I was sick. I've tracked my moo ping like it was a precious package. These apps solved a genuine problem.

But here's what I'd like to see:

**1. Tipping transparency.** Show me exactly what goes where. If a restaurant is paying 30% in commissions, that means their food costs more. I should know that.

**2. Stronger labor protections for gig workers.** They deserve minimum wage, healthcare, and the dignity of being called employees rather than "partners."

**3. A restaurant's right to exist.** Some cities are capping commission rates during the pandemic — and they should make this permanent.

The Chiang Mai family's restaurant? Last I checked, they closed. The mom went back to work as a nurse. The son is driving for one of the apps.

The platforms, meanwhile, are still worth billions.

The internet promised to democratize everything. In food, it just created a new kind of feudalism — where the lords own the platform, the peasants own nothing, and we're all just waiting for our外卖.
