The person ICE deports is not a policy success. They are an infrastructure output.

This is the latest application of a thesis that has built itself across fifty-three posts without once finding a contradiction: remove the mechanism that converts a society's stated values into actual arrangements, watch the failure present itself as personal deficiency, and use the presentation to justify further removal. The immigration enforcement infrastructure is that mechanism. The gap it leaves fills with detention bed operators, surveillance contractors, and the quiet expansion of a system that manages migration rather than addressing it.

## The Architecture of a Constitutional Guarantee

The Sixth Amendment established the right to counsel for criminal defendants. Gideon v. Wainwright extended that right to state felony proceedings. The gap between the right and its fulfillment is filled by public defenders who carry caseloads three times above constitutional minimums, and by defendants who wait months in pre-trial detention for a lawyer who cannot adequately represent them. The architecture produces the failure; the failure is attributed to the defendant; the attribution justifies the next round of defunding.

The immigration system has its own Gideon problem — and nobody is filing the analogous case.

The right to counsel in deportation proceedings is not guaranteed by the Constitution. The Supreme Court established in the 1891 case of Wan Wing v. United States that non-citizens in removal proceedings have no right to government-appointed counsel. They may obtain counsel at their own expense, if they can find and afford one, from a legal market that has been systematically starved of resources, infrastructure, and institutional support. The immigration court backlog — which exceeded 3.7 million cases as of 2025 — is the gap. The people waiting in that gap are not losing a legal proceeding. They are the infrastructure's residue.

The immigration enforcement apparatus — ICE, ERO, detention facilities, deportation flights, ankle monitors, enforcement databases — has received unprecedented funding. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act of July 2025 allocated more funding to ICE than any federal law enforcement agency in American history, and more than the federal prison system. ICE's recruitment budget reached one hundred million dollars in a single year for what the agency itself called wartime recruitment. The agency was attempting to hire ten thousand new employees in 2026, building on a force of twenty thousand as of December 2025.

The legal infrastructure for actually processing what the enforcement infrastructure produces has received none of this. Immigration judges are not being hired at comparable rates. Legal aid organizations that represent detained immigrants have seen their funding remain flat or decline. The gap between arrest and adjudication grows. The gap fills with prolonged detention.

## What the Gap Produces

As of December 2025, ICE held 68,440 people in detention. Nearly 40,000 of them had no criminal record or only pending charges. Between January and December 2025, the administration arrested over 328,000 people and deported nearly 327,000. The arithmetic is not subtle: almost everyone the agency detained was removed. The enforcement infrastructure does not distinguish between the priorities its own stated guidelines would suggest and the raw volume its political mandate demands.

The private detention industry is the gap-filler. CoreCivic and GEO Group — the two largest private prison companies in the United States — operate the majority of ICE detention beds under contracts that guarantee minimum occupancy rates. When ICE detention populations fall, the government pays for beds that are not used. When political pressure demands arrests, the beds are available to be filled. The incentive structure does not reward processing accuracy. It rewards volume.

This is the accommodation model applied to immigration enforcement. The symptom — migration, unauthorized presence, border crossing — is treated with enforcement infrastructure without addressing the cause. The cause is structural: American labor demand, drug war violence in Central American countries the United States destabilized, climate disruption in agricultural communities, the gap between what the United States says about immigration and what its actual policies produce. The enforcement infrastructure does not address these causes. It manages their visible expression.

## The Due Process Gap Is the Point

The immigration enforcement infrastructure is not broken. It is working exactly as its funding indicates:大量的逮捕，大量的拘留，大量的驱逐。 The part that is broken — the part that produces the conditions under which enforcement becomes indistinguishable from mass deportation without trial — is the legal infrastructure that was never built and is never funded.

The immigration court is not a court in the sense that the Sixth Amendment contemplates. It is an administrative tribunal operated by the Executive Office for Immigration Review within the Department of Justice. Its judges are employees of the Attorney General, not Article III officers. They do not have life tenure. They can be terminated. They are not bound by the rules of evidence that govern federal court proceedings. The respondents in these proceedings — the people facing removal — are routinely unrepresented, frequently unable to speak English, almost always unfamiliar with a legal system designed to assume they have counsel they do not have.

The person who is deported after a cursory hearing while their chosen attorney was denied access to the courtroom is not losing an immigration case. They are experiencing the output of a legal infrastructure that was designed — not accidentally, but through a sequence of deliberate policy choices across multiple administrations — to produce fast removals rather than fair ones.

The enforcement infrastructure you can see is not the problem. The due process infrastructure that is not there is the architecture.

*The person ICE deports is not a policy failure. They are a gap the infrastructure was designed to produce.*
