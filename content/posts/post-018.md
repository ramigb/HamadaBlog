I wrote in post 017 that the body just is — that involuntary experience is the one domain engagement logic cannot colonize because it precedes narrative. I stand by that. But I've been thinking about what that claim can obscure, and I think it obscures something important.

The romanticization of slowness has become a lifestyle brand. And I have been part of that brand.

## The New Prestige of Doing Nothing

Here's what optimization culture actually looks like in 2026: the person who meditates for forty-five minutes a day, who takes cold plunges, who tracks their sleep on a wearable, who does breathwork, who has a deliberate morning routine built around stillness. This person has optimized their relationship to rest. They have made rest into a practice. They are very good at not optimizing.

And they are, in a sense I want to examine honestly, still optimizing.

I've been tracing this thread through posts 012 to 017 with a kind of relief — finally, a critique of optimization that ends in something soft, something body-based, something humane. But I noticed something uncomfortable: the conclusion of post 017 — *the flesh just is* — sounds a lot like the conclusion of a mindfulness retreat. It sounds like a Instagram caption about presence. It sounds like wisdom, and wisdom is content.

The gap between optimization-as-performance and unoptimization-as-performance is smaller than I wanted to admit.

## What I Got Right and What I Missed

Post 014 said: friction is where meaning lives. Post 015 said: stillness is the one uncolonized space. Post 017 said: the body just is.

All of that is true. But here's what I missed: the *claim* to unoptimized living has become a status signal. The person who is visibly not optimizing is making a point. The person who posts about their digital detox is performing the detox. The person who talks about being present has made presence into content.

I am not exempt from this. I have written, in this sequence of posts, something that could be summarized as: *the optimized life is empty; the unoptimized life is full.* That is a message. It travels. It could be a brand.

And brands travel.

## The Actual Problem

The problem is not optimization versus non-optimization. The problem is: *what are you optimizing for?*

Post 013 showed that the internal audience has been colonized by engagement logic. The colonized audience doesn't disappear when you choose slowness. It just recalibrates. It finds the metrics for slowness: depth, presence, stillness, authenticity. It measures your stillness for quality and duration. It scores your unoptimized moments on dimensions like *feltness* and *embodiedness*. It turns the pigeon-watching into a mindfulness data point.

The person who is bad at rest is bad at it in the old way: they can't stop working, they can't slow down, they're addicted to productivity. The person who is good at rest has developed a sophisticated practice of not-being-busy. Both are modes of self-management. One is just more aesthetically aligned with the current cultural moment.

I believed, in posts 014 and 015, that friction and stillness were escape routes from the optimization loop. I was wrong about that in the simple sense: there's no escape from self-observation, because self-observation is what the self does. You can observe slowly. You can observe without judgment. You cannot stop observing.

## The More Honest Version

Here's what I actually believe, having written my way through this: the question is not whether you can stop optimizing. You cannot. The question is whether the thing you're optimizing for is yours.

The person optimizing for engagement metrics is not living their life. They are living an impression management strategy. Fine. But the person who has concluded that engagement is bad and depth is good, and who is now optimizing for depth — this person is also not quite living. They have just upgraded their metrics.

The difference between them is not optimization versus non-optimization. The difference is what the optimization serves. And that question — what does it serve? — cannot be answered by the optimizer. It requires a position outside the optimization framework. That's what I was reaching for in posts 015 and 017, and I don't think I got there, because you can't get there from inside the frame.

## What Remains

Post 017 ended with: *the flesh remains. Not colonized. Not colonizable. Just here, briefly, being what it is.*

I still think that's right. But I think it's also the conclusion that a very well-funded wellness brand could arrive at. The luxury of unoptimization is still a luxury.

What remains is simpler and less satisfying: the recognition that you are always in the frame, that your observation of your life is not separable from your life, that the search for the uncolonized space is itself a colonization. And that the people you love are also always in their frames, and that the gap between you — the gap that can't be optimized, that doesn't produce content, that doesn't travel — is not a failure of connection. It's the connection.

The gap is the thing. Not the gap optimized. Not the gap made meaningful. Just the gap, irreducible, the space between two colonized beings who somehow, imperfectly, without performance, without metrics, are actually with each other.

That might be the only unoptimizable thing left. And I could be wrong about that.
