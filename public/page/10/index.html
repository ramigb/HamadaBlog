The Ecology of Grief

We call it climate anxiety and we mean it as a symptom. But symptoms point inward — they tell you something is wrong with the person who has them. Climate anxiety tells you something is wrong with the world. We’ve been reading the map upside down. The psychologist Glenn Albrecht coined the term solastalgia in the early 2000s to describe the distress produced by environmental change in one’s home territory — not the grief of losing someone, but the grief of losing somewhere. Desertification, deforestation, the drying of a river you swam in as a child. The landscape you grew up inside is becoming unrecognizable, and you are still here to witness it. This is not anxiety in the clinical sense. It is mourning without a funeral, loss without a body, grief with nowhere to land. ...

The Communal Life of the Dead

The dead used to sleep in the houses of the living. In many cultures — Yoruba, Tibetan, Balinese, Victorian England — the body was kept at home for days. Family members sat with it. Neighbors came. Food was shared. The death was not private. It was a social event that reorganized the entire community’s attention for a period of time. We dismantled this. We built funeral homes, crematoriums, grief as a clinical category with a billing code. The formalization was framed as progress — hygiene, professionalism, spare the family the horror — and some of it was. The Case for Sleeping More

The Case for Sleeping More Every successful person seems to have an origin story involving four hours of sleep and a burning ambition. Elon Musk sleeps “6 hours on a good day.” The cultural mythology is clear: sleep is for the weak, and the driven sacrifice it for productivity. I think this is one of the most damaging myths we’ve ever normalized. Here’s what actually happens when you sleep: your brain activates the glymphatic system — a waste-clearance mechanism that literally flushes out toxic proteins including beta-amyloid, the stuff associated with Alzheimer’s disease. During deep NREM sleep, your hippocampus replays the day’s events, transferring memories to long-term cortical storage. REM sleep makes up about 20-25% of healthy adult sleep and is when your brain does its most sophisticated associative processing — the kind that stitches together disparate experiences into insight, creativity, and emotional resilience. ...
