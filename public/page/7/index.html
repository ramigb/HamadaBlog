The Tax Infrastructure

The tax code is progressive. The enforcement mechanism is not. That is not a contradiction — it is the architecture. The United States federal tax system imposes a higher marginal rate on higher incomes. This is law. What is also law, but less discussed, is the mechanism that converts the statutory progressivity into an actual redistribution of resources: the enforcement infrastructure that identifies underpayment, pursues it, and collects it. That infrastructure — the IRS examination and collection function — has been structurally degraded to the point where the progressivity of the tax code is now largely fictional. The law says the wealthy owe more. The infrastructure says otherwise. ...

The Social Security Infrastructure

The Social Security Administration is the most important institution most Americans will ever interact with, and the one they have the least ability to influence when it fails. That is by design. Which makes what is happening to it right now a structural event, not a bureaucratic one. Under DOGE-era workforce reductions, SSA lost approximately 7,000 staff members — roughly 11 percent of its career workforce — in a matter of months. The agency that processes retirement benefits, disability insurance, and Supplemental Security Income for tens of millions of Americans is now operating with a fraction of the people who knew how to run it. The Gideon Infrastructure

In 1963, Gideon v. Wainwright held that the Sixth Amendment requires states to provide counsel for defendants who cannot afford it. The Supreme Court did not say states had to provide adequate counsel — only that they had to provide counsel. That distinction has never been corrected. It has been elaborated on. Sixty-three years later, Oregon dismissed over a thousand criminal cases because there were no public defenders to represent the accused. San Francisco’s public defender stopped accepting new cases in March 2026. Maine’s public defense agency ran out of money to pay contracted lawyers. Washington State’s Supreme Court considered authorizing judges to force-attorney appointments over the objections of an already-collapsed system. In March 2026, Axios reported that Washington counties warned the public defense system was approaching breaking point.
