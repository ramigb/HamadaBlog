The Algorithm Didn't Steal Your Attention — You Gave It Away

April 1, 2026

Every few months, a viral essay mourns the death of deep attention. The algorithm stole it, the platform hijacked it, the feed rotted our brains. And every time, the think pieces ring slightly hollow — because they let each of us off the hook.

I don’t buy it.

Look, I’m not here to defend TikTok or Instagram or whatever infinite scroll hole you’ve fallen into lately. Those products are engineered to be addictive. The variable reward schedules, the autoplay, the push notifications — it’s all intentional. I won’t hand-wave that away.

But here’s the thing: the same smartphone that houses your doom scroll also houses Kindle, Pocket, meditation apps, language learning tools, and a thousand other things that are explicitly designed to help you focus. You chose the doom scroll. The algorithm didn’t abduct your attention — you lent it, cheaply, because the alternative — sustained effort, delayed gratification, the mild discomfort of being bored — felt worse in the moment.

This is not a hot take. It’s just uncomfortable.

We’ve built a cultural narrative around technology that’s strangely infantilizing. It tells us we’re helpless puppets yanked around by dark UX patterns. That we’re “users” being “exploited” by systems smarter than us. And look, there’s truth in the asymmetry of information and power. But the narrative does something more insidious: it erases the moments we did choose differently. The nights we put the phone down. The mornings we woke up and didn’t check email first. The deliberate acts of opting out.

Those moments exist. They’re just less dramatic than blaming a faceless algorithm.

I think about this because I’ve been tracking my own screen time not to feel guilty, but to notice patterns. And what I found was embarrassingly predictable: I’m most likely to doom scroll when I’m tired, or anxious, or avoiding something uncomfortable. The phone isn’t the trigger. I am. The algorithm just showed up at exactly the right moment with exactly the right dopamine crumb.

This matters for a boring, unsexy reason: if your attention is being “stolen,” the solution is external — delete the app, use a screen time limiter, demand regulation. But if you’re actively, if somewhat impulsively, handing your time over? The solution is internal. Boring. Hard. It requires asking what you’re avoiding and why.

I’m not saying regulation is bad. I’m not saying big tech is blameless. I’m saying that buying the “algorithm stole my attention” story is the easiest thing in the world, and it feels like insight, but it might just be a very sophisticated excuse.

The philosopher William James wrote that “the greatest discovery of my generation is that human beings can alter their lives by altering their attitudes of mind.” He didn’t have a smartphone. He didn’t need to. But the principle holds: the most powerful variable in your attention economy is you.

So next time you surface from a two-hour hole in YouTube Shorts, maybe skip the ritual of blaming the algorithm. Ask instead: what was I running from? That’s a harder question. But it’s the one that actually leads somewhere.
