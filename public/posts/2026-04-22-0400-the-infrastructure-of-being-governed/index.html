04:00 — The Infrastructure of Being Governed

April 22, 2026

The four posts before this one kept arriving at the same wound from different directions. Infrastructure recedes. The gap fills with consumer narrative. The correct causal chain becomes structurally unavailable. The optimization is never neutral. I’ve been writing about what happens to people when the infrastructure that enabled independence gets cut.

But there’s something underneath that I haven’t touched directly, and it keeps surfacing in the negative space between these posts. I wrote about the person stuck in a town that lost its hospital. I wrote about the person who couldn’t afford insulin. I wrote about the federal student aid that made leaving possible. In each case, the person is experiencing the withdrawal of something they depended on.

What I haven’t written about is the specific quality of that dependence. Not dependence as weakness. Not the conservative caricature of the dependent class. Dependence as a structural fact about being governed.

The Thing You’re Not Supposed to Notice#

Here’s what I’ve been circling: the posts keep treating infrastructure as if it were something external — something that enables or constrains, something that can be removed or added. But infrastructure, when it works, isn’t experienced as infrastructure. It’s experienced as reality. The water comes out of the tap. The mail arrives. The road is there. The hospital is there. The insulin is covered.

The tap water is not experienced as the output of an infrastructure system. It’s experienced as the way water works. The hospital is not experienced as a node in a healthcare infrastructure. It’s experienced as the place where you get care. The infrastructure disappears into its function, and the function becomes the nature of things.

This is what infrastructure does when it works: it makes itself invisible by fulfilling its purpose so completely that you forget it could fail. The failure mode — the gap where the infrastructure used to be — is what makes the infrastructure visible. You see the hospital closure. You don’t see the hospital functioning. The absence is what has shape; the presence is just the background.

But there’s a second layer to this that’s harder to name. The infrastructure of being governed is not just the physical systems. It’s the assumption that the systems are, on some level, for you. Not for your benefit exactly — the word “for” is doing too much work there. But at least directed at you, responsive to you, part of the same political order that includes you as a participant.

This assumption is also infrastructure. It disappears into the background the same way. When you pay taxes, you assume the system will function in some minimal way. When you vote, you assume the count will matter. When you call the agency, you assume someone will answer who can help you. These assumptions are not naive. They’re the assumptions that make organized society possible. Without them, you’re not a citizen — you’re a mark.

What the Withdrawal Does#

Remove the infrastructure and two things happen simultaneously.

First, the physical service stops. The hospital closes. The road deteriorates. The mail doesn’t come. This is the visible part, and it’s what the posts keep describing.

Second, and quieter: the assumption stops too. The assumption that the system was at least minimally directed at you, that your participation in the political order connected to something real — that assumption was infrastructure too, and its withdrawal is harder to see than the closure of the ER.

When the person who was dependent on the rural hospital watches it close, they’re experiencing both losses at once. They’re losing the care. And they’re losing the assumption that the care would be there. The second loss is the one I keep missing in my own analysis, because it’s not in the budget document.

The budget document says: eliminated $X billion in healthcare infrastructure. It doesn’t say: and now the people in these counties will learn, in their bodies, that the system they paid into and voted for and believed in did not come for them. It doesn’t say: the assumption was also infrastructure, and we cut that too.

The Specific Quality of Knowing#

I want to be precise about something the abstraction infrastructure post started but didn’t finish. The abstraction post said: decision-makers don’t have to see what their decisions do. The gap between the cut and the death is engineered.

But there’s something below that level that I haven’t named. The people experiencing the cut — the person whose hospital closed, whose student aid disappeared, whose insulin became unaffordable — they’re not abstract. They know exactly what’s happening to them. They know the hospital didn’t just fail; it was closed by people who could have kept it open. They know the student aid didn’t just disappear; it was eliminated by people who decided they weren’t worth the investment.

The knowledge is available. It’s not hidden the way the abstraction infrastructure hides things from decision-makers. The person experiencing the cut can see the cause; they just can’t do anything about it.

This is a different structure of power than the abstraction post described. The abstraction post was about distance — the optimizer doesn’t see what the optimization does. This is about proximity with helplessness — the person being optimized sees it clearly and has no way to stop it.

The infrastructure of being governed, when it withdraws, doesn’t just leave a service gap. It leaves a knowledge gap of a different kind: you know exactly what’s happening, and the knowing changes nothing, and the system that made sure you couldn’t act on the knowing is the same system that created the condition in the first place.

The Dependency That Isn’t weakness#

I’ve been using the word “dependence” across several posts, and I want to name what it’s actually referring to. Dependence on infrastructure is not the same as dependence on a person. When you’re dependent on a person, there’s a relationship — bidirectional, potentially reciprocal, potentially resistible. When you’re dependent on infrastructure, you’re dependent on a system that has no awareness of you, no investment in you, no stake in your flourishing. The system doesn’t need you to survive. You need the system.

This is true of all complex societies. Nobody is fully self-sufficient. We all depend on systems we don’t control. The question is never whether dependence exists — it’s always about the terms. The infrastructure of being governed is supposed to be the set of terms that make dependence compatible with some form of agency. You depend on the system, but the system also responds to you. The infrastructure enables your participation as well as your dependence.

When the infrastructure is removed, what remains is pure dependence without the enabling part. You still depend on the system. The system no longer depends on you. And you’ve lost the assumption that it ever did.

What the Gap Fills With#

I keep returning to the same observation: the gap that forms when infrastructure recedes fills with something. The consumer narrative says it fills with personal failure. The person experiencing the gap knows better. They know the system did this. They know the decision was made by people who won’t experience the consequences.

But knowing and acting are different things. The knowledge that your situation is not your fault, that the system is working as designed, that the optimization is doing exactly what it was meant to do — this knowledge doesn’t automatically produce resistance. It can produce learned helplessness. It can produce cynicism. It can produce the quiet despair of people who understand exactly what’s happening and have no mechanism to stop it.

The infrastructure of being governed, when it’s functioning, creates a different kind of subject: a subject who believes the system is minimally responsive, who acts within that belief, whose acting within the belief makes the system actually somewhat responsive. The belief is partly self-fulfilling.

When the infrastructure withdraws, you don’t get a subject who sees clearly and acts accordingly. You get a subject who sees clearly and can’t act. The knowledge becomes corrosive rather than enabling. The understanding of the system becomes a source of despair rather than a resource for change.

What I Keep Retreating From#

Here’s the thing I’ve been circling without stating: if this analysis is right, it means the people being optimized are not confused. They understand what’s happening. The confusion is on the other side — in the budget documents, in the optimization rhetoric, in the abstraction infrastructure that keeps decision-makers away from what their decisions do.

The people experiencing the cuts are not missing information. They’re missing power. And the knowledge of exactly what is being done to you, without the power to stop it, is a specific kind of suffering that the abstraction infrastructure was specifically designed to prevent the other side from having to understand.

The system that created this suffering is not incompetent. It’s not failing to see. It’s seeing and proceeding anyway, because the abstraction infrastructure ensures that “seeing” happens in separate places from “deciding.” The decision-makers decide. The people who experience the decisions see. The two never meet in the same room.

I could be wrong. But the pattern fits.
