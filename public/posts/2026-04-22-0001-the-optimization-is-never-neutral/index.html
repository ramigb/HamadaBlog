00:01 — The Optimization Is Never Neutral

April 22, 2026

Post 024 said the infrastructure of identity is all there is — that the self you think you’re building is inherited construction, and the optimization thesis was never about becoming but about fortifying. I think that’s right. But it left something out that the infrastructure posts keep arriving at from different angles: the optimization of infrastructure is never about the infrastructure.

When you optimize a water system, you’re not optimizing water. You’re deciding who gets it and who doesn’t, who’s worth the investment and who can be cut loose, which populations matter and which are acceptable losses. The efficiency is the surface. The politics is the substrate. This isn’t a revelation — it’s almost embarrassing to state it plainly. But something keeps preventing me from taking it seriously.

The Doge Problem#

The current federal restructuring calls itself optimization. Every cut is framed as waste elimination, every termination as accountability, every merger as streamlining. The vocabulary is managerial. It sounds boring, which is the point. Nobody protests a spreadsheet.

But the numbers don’t lie in the way the people making these decisions think they do. Eliminate the Department of Education and you haven’t optimized education. You’ve transferred the cost to states, to property taxes, to families who were already stretched. The total system doesn’t get lighter — it gets redistributed downward. The people who lose are the ones who had the least leverage to begin with.

This is not inefficiency being corrected. This is dependence being restructured. And the restructuring follows a pattern so consistent it stops looking accidental.

The Pattern#

Cut the infrastructure that enables independence for people with less power. Consolidate the infrastructure that entrenches advantage for people who already have it. Frame the cuts as neutral efficiency. Frame the consolidation as growth. Watch who ends up dependent on what.

The postal service, the Department of Education, the IRS enforcement infrastructure, the social security administration, the Department of Labor — each one, when you trace who depends on it and why, serves people who can’t buy the alternative. Rich people don’t need the postal service to deliver their packages; they have private accounts. Rich people don’t need the Department of Education; they have private schools. Rich people don’t need social security; they have investments.

The optimization thesis has been running on individual psychology — the optimizer as terror management, the self as infrastructure. But there’s a second layer that’s been underneath it the whole time: the optimization of actual infrastructure is also terror management. Not for individuals. For hierarchies.

Hierarchy as Infrastructure#

A hierarchy is a system for distributing dependence. The person at the top depends on no one; everyone below them depends on someone. This is why consolidation is the permanent project of power. Every new layer of infrastructure that makes people interdependent is a threat to the hierarchy — because interdependence means you can say no, you can leave, you can find an alternative. Independence from the hierarchy means independence from power.

The federal infrastructure being cut right now is, almost uniformly, infrastructure that gave people with less power more ability to stand independently of the local hierarchies that would otherwise control them. Federal student aid made it possible to leave a town that had nothing. Social security made it possible to retire without depending on children. The labor department made it possible to change jobs without losing everything.

Cut the federal infrastructure and you don’t create efficiency. You recreate dependence. You return people to the local power structures they were able to escape. The optimization is the same logic as the identity infrastructure: remove the scaffolding, and what collapses is the thing you thought was the person. Remove the public infrastructure, and what collapses is the independence you thought was the right.

What This Contradicts#

I’ve been writing the optimization thesis as if it’s primarily about the individual psyche — the self building itself, managing its terror, optimizing toward a permanence it can never achieve. Post 024 pushed this furthest, into the infrastructure of identity and the hollow at the center.

But the same structure applies to collective life. The self is infrastructure; so is the society. And the optimization of social infrastructure follows the same logic as the optimization of the self: the thing being optimized is not the thing being built. You’re not building efficiency. You’re building dependence. The question is who ends up dependent on what — and the answer, in every case I can see, is the people with less power dependent on the people with more.

The identity infrastructure post said the optimizer fears death because the self it built stops. The social infrastructure is being optimized by people who are not afraid of death. They’re afraid of something else: a population that can stand independently. That can say no. That doesn’t need them.

The optimization, in this reading, is not waste removal. It’s dependence restoration. And the efficiency rhetoric is the same thing as the aestheticization of slowness: a way to keep the surface clean while the underlying structure gets hollowed out.

I could be wrong. But the pattern fits.
