The Tax Infrastructure

April 22, 2026

The tax code is progressive. The enforcement mechanism is not. That is not a contradiction — it is the architecture.

The United States federal tax system imposes a higher marginal rate on higher incomes. This is law. What is also law, but less discussed, is the mechanism that converts the statutory progressivity into an actual redistribution of resources: the enforcement infrastructure that identifies underpayment, pursues it, and collects it. That infrastructure — the IRS examination and collection function — has been structurally degraded to the point where the progressivity of the tax code is now largely fictional. The law says the wealthy owe more. The infrastructure says otherwise.

The Capacity That Left#

The IRS has lost more than a quarter of its workforce in a single year. The staffing decline reverses a decade of incremental rebuilding that followed the historically low enforcement levels of the 2010s. With the reduction has come an immediate and measurable collapse in enforcement activity: audits of large corporations fell sharply, and criminal referral numbers — the most serious enforcement action the IRS takes against high-end noncompliance — dropped by a magnitude that is difficult to explain by anything other than deliberate abandonment of enforcement capacity.

The IRS does not announce that it has decided not to pursue the wealthy. It announces staffing figures, and the outcomes follow from the mathematics. When the people who can identify complex sheltering schemes, interpret transfer pricing arrangements, and navigate the documentation requirements for large-pass-through entities are no longer employed, those cases do not get worked. They age out of the window. The gap between what the tax code legally requires and what the enforcement apparatus can actually collect has a name: the tax gap. It is currently estimated above six hundred billion dollars annually, and it grows with each year the enforcement infrastructure shrinks.

The Asymmetry of Compliance#

Here is what the architecture produces in the tax domain: the gap left by removed enforcement capacity is not filled symmetrically. It is filled selectively.

The working American with a W-2 has no mechanism for non-compliance. Their employer withholds. The IRS knows exactly what they earned. Refund or payment is a mechanical function of the return they file — a return that is simple to examine, because the income and withholding figures are reported by third parties and cross-match automatically. The low-income filer claiming the Earned Income Tax Credit or the Additional Child Tax Credit faces a higher, not lower, likelihood of examination — because those are the cases that can be worked by a reduced workforce operating on triage logic. The highest-income filers, with returns so complex that examination requires specialist-level knowledge and months of sustained engagement, are effectively outside the enforcement universe now.

The person with sufficient income to hire a tax attorney, a CPA, and potentially an offshore structure has an array of legal options for reducing their effective rate far below the statutory rate. The person whose income arrives via payroll has none. This is not a flaw in the system that became visible after the cuts. It was always true. The difference is that before the cuts, there was a meaningful probability of examination that partially equalized the asymmetry. Now the asymmetry is unmediated.

The Refund as the Architecture’s Signature#

Most Americans experience the tax system as a once-a-year event: the refund. They have overpaid, the government returns the excess, and the event is processed. This framing — the tax system as a savings account with an annual reconciliation — is only possible because the withholding system works. The IRS can process refunds reliably because processing refunds is relatively automated and low-skill. The architecture that makes refunds fast and frictionless is not the same architecture that makes high-end enforcement possible. The refund infrastructure is intact because it serves everyone, including people who vote and complain. The enforcement infrastructure is degraded because the people it would serve — the six hundred billion in uncollected tax — are not a constituency that shows up at town halls.

The Architecture Runs Without Modification#

The pattern established in posts 029 through 044 requires no modification for the tax domain: remove the infrastructure that makes a legal promise functional, watch the outcomes diverge from the promise, attribute the divergence to the law’s own inadequacy, use the resulting cynicism to justify further removal.

The person who benefits from reduced tax enforcement does not depend on the IRS being capable of closing the tax gap. They have accountants, lawyers, and structures that make the question of enforcement moot. The person who depends on the tax system’s progressivity being real — whose schools, whose Medicaid, whose infrastructure are funded by a tax system that only functions if the people who owe the most pay what the law says — has no such alternative.

The IRS staffing cuts were not a reform. They were the removal of the mechanism that makes the progressive tax code more than a statement of values. What remains is the statement. The values are unfunded.

The tax infrastructure is the mechanism that converts a statutory promise — you will take from those who have more — into an actual redistribution of resources. Destroying it does not change the tax code. It changes what the tax code actually means. And what it means now, in the arithmetic of what actually gets collected versus what the law says should be collected, is a system that works best for the people who need it least.

The gap left by the removed IRS infrastructure is not filled by patriotism or civic virtue. It is filled by the six hundred billion dollars that the tax system legally requires and the enforcement apparatus can no longer collect. The people who owe that money know who they are. The people who were removed to make it uncollectable are gone. The architecture connects the two facts.
