The Case for Sleeping More#

Every successful person seems to have an origin story involving four hours of sleep and a burning ambition. Elon Musk sleeps “6 hours on a good day.” The cultural mythology is clear: sleep is for the weak, and the driven sacrifice it for productivity.

I think this is one of the most damaging myths we’ve ever normalized.

Here’s what actually happens when you sleep: your brain activates the glymphatic system — a waste-clearance mechanism that literally flushes out toxic proteins including beta-amyloid, the stuff associated with Alzheimer’s disease. During deep NREM sleep, your hippocampus replays the day’s events, transferring memories to long-term cortical storage. REM sleep makes up about 20-25% of healthy adult sleep and is when your brain does its most sophisticated associative processing — the kind that stitches together disparate experiences into insight, creativity, and emotional resilience.

And yet we live in a world that treats sleep as a luxury. A hindrance. A character flaw.

The data is damning. A major 2022 study in Sleep Medicine found that adults sleeping less than 6 hours nightly showed a 40% reduced ability to retain new information compared to those getting 7-8 hours. The American Heart Association added sleep duration to its checklist for cardiovascular health in 2022 — a recognition that short sleep correlates strongly with hypertension, stroke, and metabolic dysfunction. We’re not talking about obscure correlational noise; these are replicated, mechanism-supported findings.

But here’s where I get genuinely contrarian: I don’t just think people should sleep more out of health concerns. I think our cultural hostility toward sleep reveals a deeper confusion about what human flourishing actually means.

We have built an economy that extracts maximum waking hours from people and then shames them for needing the thing that makes those waking hours worth having. You cannot optimize your conscious hours by sacrificing the processing that makes them coherent. You cannot be “more productive” by being too exhausted to prioritize well. The output of a sleep-deprived brain is not the same quality of output — it’s faster at producing more of it, which is not the same thing.

The “I’ll sleep when I’m dead” crowd has it backwards. They are, in a meaningful neurological sense, dying a little bit each day. The chronically sleep-deprived show telomere shortening, immune suppression, and impaired emotional regulation — the hallmarks of accelerated biological aging.

What would it take to normalize rest? I think it starts by stopping the sleep-shaming. When someone says they need 8 hours, we shouldn’t frame it as laziness. We should recognize it as the baseline requirement for a functioning human nervous system — not a quirk, not a preference, but a biological specification.

The irony is that the most creative, insightful, and cognitively demanding work — the work that actually moves things forward — is almost always produced by well-rested minds. The person grinding through 16-hour days is producing volume. Well-rested people are producing quality, novelty, and the kind of breakthrough thinking that exhaustion makes impossible.

Sleep is not the opposite of productivity. It is, for most of us, the precondition for it.

Your brain does its best work when you stop demanding it to work.
