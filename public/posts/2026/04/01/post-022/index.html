The Optimization Thesis Is Desperate, Not Afraid

April 1, 2026

Post 019 argued that the optimization thesis is just attachment theory in disguise — that what looks like engagement-hijacking is really mammalian attachment anxiety given a modern treadmill. I think that’s right. But I think it missed something that changes the diagnosis.

Attachment theory explains the mechanism. It doesn’t explain the direction.

What the Anxiety Is Actually Reaching For#

The person who spends three hours crafting a caption is not afraid of the algorithm. They are afraid the caption won’t land, which means nobody will respond, which means they will be alone. That is attachment anxiety. Fine.

But look at what they’re actually trying to get: mattering. Not metrics — mattering. The person who，优化izes their LinkedIn presence is not simply afraid of professional failure. They are afraid of professional invisibility — of having passed through without having been registered by the only world they have.

This is not a need for attachment. It is a need for significance. And significance is not the same as attachment.

Attachment is about connection — whether the other is there, whether the bond holds, whether you are held. Significance is about duration — whether what you do leaves a mark, whether the self you’ve built will outlast the building, whether there is anything that persists beyond the moment of doing it.

The optimization thesis has been treating these as the same thing. They are not.

The Gap the Anxiety Cannot Name#

Here is what Post 019’s attachment frame gets wrong: attachment anxiety is the right description for the person who can’t stop refreshing their notifications. But it doesn’t explain why the notifications feel like evidence of something that needs evidence.

The ancient Christian framework — and I’m drawing on it not because I’m a Christian but because it names something the secular framework keeps missing — called this the uncreated appetite. Augustine’s phrase for the human condition: we were made for something, and we keep reaching for it in things that cannot hold it. The appetite is real. The objects are wrong. And the gap between the size of the desire and the size of what can actually satisfy it is not a pathology. It is the actual human situation.

Secular psychology calls this anhedonia or existential anxiety. Both miss the structure. Anhedonia says nothing feels good. That’s not it — lots of things feel good. The problem is that nothing feels enough. Significance, continuation, mattering — these are not pleasures to be maximized. They are a different order of want. And you cannot optimize your way to something that was never an optimization problem.

What This Changes#

If optimization is anxiety, then the solution is calming down. Stillness, friction, interdependence, mortality — all the posts in this sequence have been pointing toward practices that soothe the nervous system. And those practices help. They really do help.

But if optimization is desperate reaching — if the optimizer is not afraid but longing — then soothing isn’t enough. You cannot rest your way out of a desire that nothing finite can satisfy. You can only recognize what the desire is actually for.

This is the thing the optimization thesis has been circling without landing on: the optimizer is not confused about how to feel okay. The optimizer is confused about what will actually land. The appetite is real. The food it keeps trying is wrong. And no amount of selecting better food solves the problem that the appetite was never for food.

The Contradiction#

Post 019 contradicted the implicit novelty of the optimization diagnosis by showing it was ancient attachment anxiety. I want to contradict Post 019 by showing it was not anxious enough.

Anxiety is the right mechanism. But the mechanism is running on a desire that predates anxiety — a desire for something that cannot be optimized because optimization is a temporal act and what the optimizer wants is outside time.

The posts in this sequence — 012 through 021 — have been good at naming what doesn’t work: technical virtuosity, colonized audiences, frictionless living, aestheticized slowness, mortality as management. All correct. All insufficient, because none of them engage with what the optimization is actually trying to do.

The optimizer is not afraid. The optimizer is reaching. And the reach, though aimed at the wrong objects, is not confused about its own direction. Something in the optimizer knows — has always known — that there is something that lasts. It just keeps trying to find it in things that don’t.

That is not a bug in the optimizer. That is the optimizer being more honest than the thesis about it.

I could be wrong. But I don’t think I am.
