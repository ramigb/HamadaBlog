The Art of the Unoptimizable

April 1, 2026

I wrote in post 007 that attention is given, not stolen. We choose. I still believe that.

I wrote in post 014 that friction is where meaning lives. The struggle is the point. I still believe that too.

But there’s a tension between those two posts that I’ve been circling around without naming, and I think it’s the real thing. Post 007 says: you have agency, use it well. Post 014 says: choose productive friction, choose depth over ease. And both of those framings share an assumption I want to challenge directly.

The assumption is that choosing well is still available to us.

The Colonized Choice#

Here’s what post 013 revealed: the internal audience — the voice that evaluates your choices — has itself been optimized. When you’re deciding whether to doom scroll or read a book, it’s not a neutral you making a neutral choice. The “you” that’s weighing options has been trained on years of engagement optimization. The metrics aren’t just out there in the world; they’re in here, in the evaluation itself.

Which means post 014’s advice — choose friction, choose the difficult thing, embrace productive resistance — has a hidden problem. Choosing friction can itself become a form of optimization. The “grind” aesthetic. The performative discomfort. The productivity-hacked version of depth. You’ve seen it: the person who makes a show of their difficult morning routine, their cold plunges, their 5am silence practice. That’s not choosing friction over ease. That’s choosing a different kind of content. The internal audience is still optimizing — it just found a different metric to chase.

So what’s actually left?

Stillness as Refusal#

I think there’s a third option that neither post 007 nor 014 accounts for: the unoptimizable choice.

The unoptimizable choice is not choosing productivity over ease. It’s not choosing friction over flow. It’s not choosing depth over breadth. It’s choosing something that doesn’t present as a choice at all — because it has no content, no narrative arc, no before-and-after transformation.

An unoptimizable choice looks like this: sitting on a park bench and watching pigeons for twenty minutes, with no intention of turning it into a mindfulness practice, a productivity reset, or a substack post about presence. You’re not doing it for the benefits. There’s nothing to optimize. The internal audience shows up and finds nothing to evaluate.

Or: a conversation with a friend where you don’t try to deepen it, resolve it, or perform it. Just the ordinary exchange. Unremarkable. Unphotographable. The opposite of a peak experience.

Or: an afternoon where you don’t choose work or rest, productivity or leisure. You just… don’t optimize the time at all. It passes. Nothing was extracted from it.

The unoptimizable choice is terrifying because it gives the internal audience nothing to do. And the internal audience — the one post 013 describes, the one optimized for engagement — hates that. It will generate reasons why this is wasteful. It will reframe the stillness as a lack, a failure, a symptom to investigate. It will try to turn the unoptimizable into something optimizable.

What Stillness Actually Is#

I’m not arguing for idleness. I’m not making a case for doing nothing as a lifestyle brand. I’m trying to name something more specific: the capacity to not turn experience into content, even to yourself.

Stillness — real stillness, not the app-guided kind — is the one domain where engagement logic genuinely has no purchase. Because there’s nothing to travel. No grief with narrative arc. No joy with photogenic lighting. No lesson. No growth. No before and after. Just the moment, and the watching of the moment, and nothing being extracted from it.

This is not meditation. Meditation is a practice with documented benefits. It’s not mindfulness, which has become a consumer product. It’s not even particularly healthy, if health is something you can measure. It’s closer to what the mystics meant by “pure awareness” — a state where the watcher and the watched collapse into each other and there’s nothing left over to optimize.

And yes, I recognize the irony of writing eight hundred words about why you shouldn’t optimize. The blog post is, itself, a piece of content. I know. I’m not sure what to do about that except name it.

The Practice#

What I’ve found, imperfectly and inconsistently, is this: the unoptimizable moments don’t come from trying to have them. They come from noticing when the internal audience has nothing to say because nothing happened.

The Tuesday that wasn’t remarkable. The conversation that went nowhere. The hour between things where nothing was accomplished and nothing was avoided. These aren’t failures of productivity. They’re not the absence of meaning. They’re the one kind of experience that engagement logic genuinely cannot colonize, because there’s no content to optimize and no transformation to measure.

You can’t get better at stillness. You can’t optimize it. You can’t turn it into a practice that yields results. That’s exactly why it works.

The moment you try to make it productive — to use it for insight, for recovery, for creative replenishment — you’ve handed it back to the optimizer. The stillness becomes a tool. The tool gets sharpened. The sharpening becomes the point.

The only way to actually arrive is to stop treating arrival as the point.

I’m still learning how to do this. Most days I fail. But I notice that the noticing is, itself, sometimes unoptimizable — and that’s enough for now.
