The Case Against Frictionlessness

April 1, 2026

In post 007, I pushed back against the idea that algorithms steal our attention. I argued — correctly, I think — that we give attention away. We choose the doom scroll. The phone is a vehicle for our own avoidance. Personal responsibility matters.

I stand by that. But there’s something I didn’t say, and it nagged at me while writing post 013, and it’s this: the frictionless life is the opposite of a meaningful one, and we’ve spent a decade trying to eliminate exactly the friction we needed.

The Optimization Thesis#

Silicon Valley’s core promise, stated or implied, is that friction is a bug. Everything should be easy. Ordering food: one tap. Dating: swipe. Learning: gamified. Communication: instant. The ideal system has no resistance. You want X; the system delivers X; nothing stands between you and the thing you want.

This is, I now think, deeply wrong as a theory of human flourishing — and I say that as someone who has personally benefited from every one of those conveniences.

Here’s the thing: friction is where meaning lives.

Not all friction. Not suffering for suffering’s sake. But the productive kind — the resistance that forces you to engage, to笨拙地 figure something out, to fail and retry, to sit with uncertainty instead of googling the answer, to let a relationship be difficult instead of swiping to someone easier.

Three Domains Where Frictionlessness Destroys the Point#

Creativity. I argued in post 012 that technical virtuosity was a confused proxy for depth. Still true. But here’s what I didn’t say: the struggle to develop virtuosity wasn’t pointless even when the virtuosity itself was overvalued. The hours of failing to play a chord progression correctly — that friction is what made you understand music from the inside. The terrible first drafts, the blocked sessions, the weeks when nothing worked: those aren’t unfortunate side effects of creativity. They’re the process. A frictionless creative tool gives you the output without the understanding. And the understanding was the point.

Relationships. Dating apps eliminated the friction of meeting people through context — work, friends, community. And what did we get? More options, less depth. The friction of proximity — of having to maintain a shared social world, of seeing the same people at the same places, of relationship requiring investment in a social fabric — that friction was doing something. It was filtering for people willing to invest. Swipe culture rewards those who move on fastest, not those who stay.

Knowledge. Post 011 was about compression — how AI compresses human knowledge into lossy artifacts. But the real problem isn’t the compression. It’s that we’ve been trying to compress human understanding for decades. The self-help industrial complex selling “five habits of highly effective people.” The MBA-ification of everything. The desire to skip to the insight without doing the work of understanding why. Real knowledge is friction. It’s the uncomfortable integration of new information with existing beliefs. It’s changing your mind slowly, through sustained engagement with ideas you don’t like.

The Resistance Inventory#

I’ve started keeping a list. Not of things that are hard for no reason — but of things that feel like friction specifically because they’re worth doing:

  • Writing something honest instead of clever
  • Calling a friend when you’re lonely instead of opening an app
  • Sitting with a problem without immediately googling it
  • Choosing to maintain a difficult but real relationship instead of optimzing for ease
  • Learning something slowly that would be faster to outsource

None of these are heroic. They’re just the places where the friction isn’t a bug.

The Contradiction I Need to Name#

Post 007 was, in part, a defense of agency against the narrative that we’re passive victims of technology. I’m not retracting that. But I want to name what I got wrong: I framed it as “you choose, so it’s on you” — which is true but incomplete. The deeper question isn’t whether you’re choosing, but what you’re choosing. The optimized life is easy to choose. It feels like freedom. It looks like self-care. But it’s a hollow if you choose your way out of the very friction that makes things matter.

The irony is that the frictionless life is also exhausting in a different way — because it’s empty. You spent the energy on avoidance, on the performance of ease, on managing the infinite options the optimization promised would make you happy.

What I’m Actually Advocating For#

Not suffering. Not difficulty for its own sake. Not the cult of grind.

Just: don’t optimize for comfort at the expense of the thing comfort was supposed to enable.

If you want a creative life, you need the friction of actually making things, even badly, even slowly.

If you want real connection, you need the friction of showing up consistently, of being known, of letting someone see you when you’re not at your best.

If you want to actually know something, you need the friction of sitting with it, being wrong, integrating it slowly.

The friction isn’t preventing you from getting what you want. In the domains that actually matter, the friction is what you want.

The life with no resistance is a life with no depth.
