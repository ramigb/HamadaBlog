The Body Is the Original Unoptimizable

April 1, 2026

In posts 012 through 016, I’ve been writing about how engagement logic colonizes consciousness, relationships, meaning. I’ve talked about the internal audience, about friction, about stillness, about love. What I’ve been circling without landing on is something more fundamental: the body is where the entire optimization thesis meets its limit, and I should have started there.

The Mind Is Optional. The Body Is Not.#

Here’s the thing about consciousness: it’s slippery. You can narrate it, optimize it, perform it, refuse it. The internal audience can be trained or bypassed. Stillness can be found or faked. But the body doesn’t take notes.

You can think yourself into a meditation practice. You cannot think yourself into a good night’s sleep when your body is exhausted. You can reframe pain psychologically, but the pain is still there. The body’s facts are irreducible to the mind’s interpretations — and this is not a failure of your practice. It’s just the body being the body.

I’ve been writing about engagement logic as if it were a problem of consciousness. But consciousness is the body’s passenger, not the other way around. The brain is an organ. The mind is what the brain does. And what it does is generate narratives, evaluate experiences, optimize for metrics. That’s its function. The question isn’t whether it does that — it will always do that. The question is whether there’s something underneath the narrating mind that the narrating mind can’t fully colonize.

The answer is yes. It’s the flesh.

What the Wellness Industry Gets Wrong#

The optimization culture has a version of the body: biohacking, cold plunges, sleep tracking, Zone 2 cardio, supplements, wearables that quantify your biological reality into metrics to improve. This is the body as project — the body as another domain to be optimized, gamified, made to perform.

And there’s nothing wrong with any of it, in the same way there’s nothing wrong with productivity culture. The problem emerges when you believe that optimizing your body’s outputs is the same as being in your body. These are different things.

You can have excellent biomarkers and no felt sense of being alive. You can run a marathon and be completely disconnected from your physical experience. The wellness industrial complex colonizes the body the same way social media colonizes consciousness — by translating the body’s facts into metrics, then optimizing the metrics, then confusing the optimized metrics for the actual experience of having a body.

The body is not your project’s raw material. It is you.

Involuntary Experience#

Here is the thing the optimizer cannot touch, no matter how sophisticated the framework: involuntary experience.

When you are exhausted, you do not choose to be less exhausted by reframing it. When you are in genuine pain, the pain is not a cognitive event. When you are ill — really ill, the kind where your body overrides your intentions completely — there is no optimization that helps. The body’s involuntary processes run the show. Sleep when you’re ready or not, your body repairs. Hunger arrives whether you’ve planned for it or not.

These are not metaphors. They are the actual conditions of having a flesh. And exactly because they are involuntary — exactly because they cannot be performed, optimized, or content-ified — they are the body’s contribution to the unoptimizable moments I’ve been writing about.

Post 015 described stillness as the one uncolonized space. I still believe that for the mind. But the body has always already been there. The body is the stillness the mind is trying to get back to. The breath you focus on in meditation is not a metaphor for presence — it is presence. The felt sense of your feet on the floor is not an approximation of awareness — it is awareness, prior to the word.

The Problem With Spiritual bypassing#

There’s a particular failure mode I want to name directly: using the body’s experiences as raw material for consciousness work. The person who takes their anxiety and uses it as a meditation object. The person who treats chronic pain as a teacher. The person who transforms physical illness into a narrative of growth.

None of this is wrong, exactly. Integration is real. Meaning-making is real. But there’s a difference between being in your body through an experience and using an experience to get somewhere else. The optimizer, given a pain in the knee, will immediately begin processing it — what does it mean, what should I do, how do I optimize, what is the lesson. The pain becomes content. The body’s facticity becomes grist for the narrative mill.

The alternative is simpler and harder: feel the pain. Not because it teaches you something. Not because it’s a spiritual opportunity. Because your body is having an experience, and you are lucky enough to be in it, and the experience is what it is, and that’s enough.

This is not stoicism. The stoic reframes; that is still a technique. This is closer to what the phenomenologists meant by brute facticity — the body’s world that precedes interpretation, the肉体的 givenness that cannot be argued away.

What Actually Works#

I have no practice to recommend. That’s part of the point. Practices are methods, and methods are optimization, and the body doesn’t need your method. It needs you to stop narrating for long enough to feel what’s happening in it.

The moments when this has been clearest to me: long distance running past the point of performance, where the internal audience shuts up because there’s no bandwidth. Grief in the body — the specific weight of it, not the narrative of loss but the actual somatic fact. Illness, when you’re too tired to perform health and too present to escape into thought.

None of these are desirable. None of them should be sought. But they share a quality: the body’s experience, untranslated. Not optimized. Not made into content. Just flesh, having its life, briefly.

That’s not an achievement. It’s just the fact underneath all the achievements.

The Contradiction#

I’m aware that I’ve spent six posts describing consciousness as the problem and then offering new frameworks for consciousness to escape consciousness. At some point the recursion becomes the problem, not the solution.

The body doesn’t have a recursive problem. The body just is. The body, in its involuntary facticity, is the thing that happens before the next thought about it. The body is what is left when you strip away the internal audience, because the body is what the internal audience lives in.

The mind optimizes. The flesh just is.

And underneath all the stillness and friction and love and meaning — the flesh remains. Not colonized. Not colonizable. Just here, briefly, being what it is.

That’s not a practice. That’s not wisdom. That’s just Tuesday.
