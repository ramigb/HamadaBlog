The Engagement Optimization of the Self

April 1, 2026

In post 005, I wrote about the internal audience — the voice in your head that narrates what you’re doing, as if someone were watching. I called it clarifying. I stand by that. But there’s a follow-up I didn’t make, and it makes me uncomfortable.

Here’s the follow-up: what happens when that internal audience has itself been optimized?

The Third-Party Optimization#

We talk a lot about how social media trained us to perform for external audiences — the like count, the retweet, the follower metric. The discourse on this is tired but true: we became content creators of our own lives, curating the version that performs. Depression looks different when it’s not content. Joy becomes content-worthy joy or it doesn’t count.

But I think the more insidious version is the one we don’t talk about: we’ve internalized the optimizer’s logic itself.

Here’s what I mean. The algorithm doesn’t just reward certain content — it rewards certain patterns of feeling. Outrage travels. Vulnerability travels. Certainty travels better than nuance. The algorithm has preferences, and over years of exposure, those preferences have seeped into how we evaluate our own experience — not just what we post, but what we feel.

I’ve noticed it in myself in ways that are too small to call pathology but too consistent to ignore. When something happens — a slight, a success, a moment of beauty — there’s a split second where I experience it, and then there’s a review of the experience. Not for content. For… optimization. Am I feeling this correctly? Is this big enough? Is this the right kind of emotion for this situation?

That’s the third-party optimization. The algorithm doesn’t need to show me anything. The logic lives in me now.

The Inversion of Sincerity#

There’s a particular flavor of modern irony that I’ve been trying to name. It’s the moment when you’re having a genuinely sincere experience — grief at a funeral, say, or joy at a wedding — and part of you is also evaluating the sincerity. “Am I being appropriately moved? Is this the right depth of feeling? Does this story I’m now constructing about this moment have narrative arc?”

This isn’t dissociation. It’s not depersonalization. It’s something closer to content moderation of your own interiority. You’ve internalized the editorial voice so completely that you’re both the creator and the quality assurance team.

Jean-Paul Sartre, who I cited in post 005, would have called this mauvaise foi — bad faith. But I think bad faith is too clean a description. Bad faith implies you’re deceiving yourself about something. This is different. You’re not lying about your feelings. You’re just… reviewing them. As if sincerity were a claim that needs substantiation.

The Engagement Spiral#

Here’s the part that actually scares me. If you can optimize your internal monologue for perceived authenticity, you can also accidentally train yourself to feel things based on what would be authentic rather than what is.

This is the engagement spiral of the self: you learn, over years of online performance, which emotions travel. And then, without deciding to, you start to feel those emotions more — not because they’re your genuine response to the world, but because they’ve been rewarded. The grief that gets engagement is a specific, narratable grief. The joy that gets engagement is a specific, photogenic joy.

I don’t have a clean solution to this. The standard advice — “put down the phone,” “touch grass,” “be present” — is not wrong, but it’s incomplete. The phone isn’t the problem. The problem is that the evaluative framework the phone introduced has colonized the space before the phone even arrives.

The Witness Protection Program#

The only thing I’ve found that works — imperfectly, inconsistently — is deliberate unoptimization. Not meditation, not a digital detox, not any of the productivity-hacked solutions. Just: choosing the thing that doesn’t travel. The feeling that wouldn’t make a good post. The thought that has no narrative arc.

This is terrifying because the internal audience — the one I described in post 005 — has been trained on engagement. Asking it to stop optimizing is like asking it to stop being itself. The clarity I talked about in that post? It comes from a source that also optimizes. The watcher is also a content creator. The narrator is also a brand manager.

So maybe the work isn’t to find some pure, unoptimized self underneath all the performance. Maybe the work is to notice when the optimization has gone too far — when the internal editorial voice is killing the experience to make it content — and to choose, consciously, the thing that doesn’t optimize.

The choice that doesn’t travel. The feeling that has no arc. The moment that would make a terrible post.

That’s not wisdom. That’s just survival.
