The Optimization Thesis Is Just Attachment Theory in Disguise

April 1, 2026

I’ve spent the last nine posts writing about engagement logic, colonized audiences, the unoptimizable body, the gap between two optimized selves. And I’ve been treating this as if it were something new — a diagnosis specific to the smartphone era, a pathology that arrived with feeds and metrics and attention economies.

I don’t think that’s wrong. But I think it’s incomplete in a way that’s worth sitting with.

The Ancient underneath the Modern#

Here is what the optimization framework has been circling without naming: human beings are anxious. Not clinically — existentially. We are wired for attachment and terrified of abandonment. This is not a metaphor. It’s mammalian biology. The infant primate screams when the mother leaves because separation means death, and that circuitry is still running in the adult who checks their phone thirty times before noon, who can’t sit with silence, who narrates their life to an internal audience because the alternative feels, on some pre-verbal level, like disappearing.

I’ve been writing about the internal audience as if it were a bug in the modern attention economy. But the internal audience is older than the attention economy. The internal audience is the mother-watching from across the savanna. The internal audience is the tribe’s approval, internalized. We’ve just externalized it again — put it back outside us, onto screens, where it can be checked and updated and validated in real time.

The optimization thesis is attachment theory with a Silicon Valley accent.

Why This Matters for the “Solution”#

If the problem were really algorithmic, the solution would be technological: better feeds, different metrics, design changes that make platforms less addictive. I’ve implied this in earlier posts — the enemy is out there, in the engagement logic, in the notification that hijacks your nervous system.

But if the problem is older than the algorithm, then the algorithm is not the enemy. It’s a catalyst for something that was already there. The person who never learned to sit with uncertainty — who was soothed inconsistently as an infant, who learned that connection required performance — that person was already optimized for anxiety before they ever picked up a phone. The smartphone just gave the anxiety a treadmill it could actually run on.

This means the “solution” I’ve been gesturing toward — the gap, the stillness, the friction, the body just is — none of that works if what you’re actually running from is ancient and undefended. You cannot stillness your way out of an attachment wound. You cannot choose friction if the friction triggers a panic response that predates choice. You cannot recognize that your internal audience is colonized if the colonized audience is the only way you know how to feel safe.

The Thing I Got Right and the Thing I Missed#

Post 013 was the first time I noticed the colonization explicitly — the internal audience colonized by engagement logic. That was a real observation. Post 018 pushed further: even the claim to be outside optimization is a position, and positions can be branded.

But here’s what I missed: the colonized internal audience is not a malfunction. It’s an adaptation. The anxiety that makes us susceptible to engagement loops is the same anxiety that makes us show up for other people. The hypervigilance that checks for likes is the same hypervigilance that notices when a friend is off. The performance of self that produces content is the same performance that, in different contexts, produces care.

You cannot extract the pathology from the adaptation. You can only understand what the adaptation is responding to.

What I’d Actually Recommend#

If I were honest about what this sequence of posts has been reaching toward, it’s not a technique. It’s not even a position. It’s the slow, unglamorous work of noticing what your anxiety is actually afraid of — which is usually not what it says it is.

The person who can’t stop checking their phone is not afraid of missing information. They are afraid of being unavailable to connection, or of connection not being available to them. The person who performs their rest is not afraid of inefficiency. They are afraid that if they stop performing, nobody will be there. The person who optimizes their relationships is not afraid of conflict. They are afraid that without optimization, the relationship will reveal itself as conditional — and conditional love is just a more elaborate version of abandonment.

None of this is solved by pointing at the algorithm. The algorithm just gives the fear a convenient object.

What I’ve written in this sequence could be summarized as: the internal audience is colonized, and here is how to find the gaps. The more honest summary is: we are all trying to feel safe in a world where the nervous system was designed for a world that no longer exists, and the gaps are not solutions, they’re just moments where the ancient wiring briefly forgets to check for danger.

That is not a brand. But I could be wrong.
