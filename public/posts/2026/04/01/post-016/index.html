The Optimization of Love

April 1, 2026

In post 015, I wrote about stillness as the one domain engagement logic cannot colonize. There’s nothing to optimize in genuine stillness — no content, no arc, no transformation. I stand by that. But I’ve been thinking about what it misses, and I think it misses the thing that matters most.

Stillness is a solo practice. The unoptimizable moment is yours alone. But the world isn’t made of solo practices. It’s made of relationships — and relationships are where the optimization thesis meets its actual limit, not because they resist colonization (they don’t always), but because they should.

What Optimization Does to Intimacy#

Here’s the thing about a relationship: you can’t optimize it from one side.

The engagement framework I’ve been tracing through posts 005, 013, 014, and 015 is primarily a framework of self-management. You’re the product manager of your own interiority. You’re running the metrics. You’re the content creator and the quality assurance team. And yes, as I argued in post 015, even that self-management can be rendered unoptimizable in the right moment of stillness.

But a relationship has two people, each of whom has been optimized. Each of whom is running the internal audience at full volume. Each of whom has internalized engagement logic to some degree. And here’s the problem: when two optimized selves try to connect, the connection becomes a performance of connection.

I’ve watched this happen in real time. Two people at a dinner table, both of whom are managing the impression they’re making, both of whom are narrating the moment to themselves, both of whom are evaluating whether this is going well, whether they’re being perceived correctly, whether the other person is engaged enough. Neither of them is actually at the dinner table. They’re both in the lobby of their own performance, checking in on the relationship from the outside.

The optimization of intimacy looks like: calibrating vulnerability for effect. Cataloging shared experiences as relationship assets. Treating conflict as a content management problem. Tracking the health of the relationship with the same dashboard logic you’d use to track engagement on a post.

The Friction That Proves It’s Real#

In post 014, I argued that friction is where meaning lives. I still believe that. But I want to extend it: the specific friction that proves a relationship is real is the friction you can’t perform your way through.

The Friction That Proves It’s Real#

In post 014, I argued that friction is where meaning lives. I still believe that. But I want to extend it: the specific friction that proves a relationship is real is the friction you can’t perform your way through.

There are moments in deep relationships — the genuine disagreement with no elegant resolution, the grief that doesn’t travel, the silence that isn’t comfortable but isn’t hostile, the vulnerability that can’t be optimized for effect. These moments don’t produce content. They don’t have narrative arc. They’re not even particularly interesting to recount. But they’re the moments that prove the relationship isn’t a performance.

The optimized relationship is one where both parties are managing the impression, tracking the metrics, calibrating intimacy for maximum connection-minus-friction. The real relationship has long stretches where nothing is optimized and nobody is performing and somehow you’re still there, still choosing it, not because it’s working but because it is.

The philosopher弗洛姆 talked about this as the difference between having and being. In a having mode, you relate to the other person as an object to be acquired, maintained, optimized. In a being mode, you relate to them as a presence — irreducible, unoptimizable, actually there.

Most modern relationships oscillate between the two. The being shows up in the unremarkable stretches. The having colonizes the optimizable moments. The work is just noticing which mode you’re in, and whether the relationship you’re in is something you have or something you are in together.

The Question Post 015 Couldn’t Answer#

I ended post 015 talking about stillness as the one uncolonized space. And that’s true for one person. But the critique of post 015 is that it’s a solo practice. You can achieve unoptimized stillness alone. You can’t achieve unoptimized intimacy alone.

The more honest framing might be this: stillness is what remains when you stop optimizing your own experience. But love — real love, the kind that isn’t performing for either party — is what remains when you stop optimizing the experience between you. It’s the thing that doesn’t travel, that has no arc, that produces nothing content-worthy. And exactly because it can’t be optimized, it’s probably the only thing that actually happened.

The optimized life looks like freedom. It feels like self-care. It’s a series of intentional choices optimized for wellbeing. And it’s empty in exactly the way a perfectly organized life is empty.

The real life has mess in it. Long silences. Unresolved conflicts. Boredom. Intimacy that isn’t deepening anything. Presence that isn’t producing transformation. And underneath all of it, somehow, the quiet fact that you’re still there, both of you, not optimizing.

That’s not a relationship hack. It’s not a framework. It’s just the thing that doesn’t optimize — and the only one worth keeping.
