The Optimizer Is Already Dead

April 1, 2026

I’ve been writing for ten posts about what can’t be optimized. Stillness, the body, interdependence, the gap between two colonized selves. I’ve been circling something I haven’t named directly, and I think it’s the thing underneath all of it.

Here it is: you are going to die. Not as an abstraction. As a fact. And the optimizer does not know what to do with this.

The Year the Calendar Became Personal#

There’s a moment in early adulthood — different for everyone, but recognizable when it arrives — when you first calculate your age against the time you have left. Not in the abstract way children understand death (it happens to grandparents, eventually), but as your own trajectory. You look at the number of years behind you and the reasonable estimate of years ahead, and something shifts.

That shift is the end of the optimizer’s fantasy.

The optimizer operates on the assumption that the work is linear and infinite: you identify what’s wrong, you apply the intervention, you measure the improvement, you continue. This is the logic of the dashboard, the codebase, the five-year plan. It assumes a future that resembles the present, tasks that can be completed, and a self that persists to enjoy the results.

Mortality collapses the assumption of persistence. And once you know you’re going to die — really know it, in the way that changes your nervous system rather than just your philosophy — optimization starts to look not wrong, but absurd. Not because optimization is bad, but because it’s aimed at a moving target that stops moving.

What You’re Actually Doing When You Optimize#

I’ve spent this entire sequence arguing that optimization colonizes experience — that the optimized life is a performed life, a life managed for metrics that don’t capture what actually matters. That argument is still right. But I think I missed why we find optimization so compelling in the first place.

The optimizer is trying to make something last. Not just a body (though that’s part of it — the supplements, the exercise, the sleep tracking) but a self. A version of yourself that improves over time, that becomes more capable, more wise, more worthy of the life you’ve been given. The optimization frame treats your current self as a rough draft to be edited, your future self as a product to be shipped.

This is not cynical. It’s anxious. And the anxiety underneath it is not, ultimately, about productivity or performance or even belonging. It’s about the discovery, arriving at different times for different people, that none of this continues.

Death as the Original Unoptimizable#

I’ve been arguing through posts 014 to 020 that there are domains that resist optimization: friction, stillness, the body, interdependence. These are real. But I think I buried the lead. The original unoptimizable — the one that makes all the others legible — is the simple fact of finitude.

You cannot optimize death out of the equation. You cannot improve your way past it. You cannot manage your relationship to it so effectively that it becomes comfortable. Every technique for "making peace with mortality" is, in the end, a technique for not thinking about it — which is different from having resolved it.

And here’s what I keep arriving at: the reason the unoptimizable domains matter is precisely because they are the ones where you stop pretending you have time. Stillness is uncolonizable because there is nothing to produce in it — and production requires a future self to consume the product. Interdependence is unoptimizable because you cannot control another person’s choices — and control requires a future in which your preferences persist. The body just is because the body is the thing that will stop being.

The Uncomfortable Implication#

I’ve tried, throughout this sequence, to end in places that felt like resolution. The gap between two colonized selves. The friction that proves a relationship is real. The flesh that just is. I meant all of it.

But here’s what mortality does to those conclusions: it makes them urgent rather than peaceful. Post 015 argued that stillness is the one uncolonized space. That’s true. But the reason you want that uncolonized space is not because stillness is aesthetically preferable to optimization. It’s because you are running out of time to be in it.

The same fact — you will die — makes interdependence matter more, not less. Not because love is an achievement to be optimized before the deadline. But because the window is real, and it’s closing, and the person you’re in it with is also closing, and somehow the fact that it’s temporary is exactly what makes it yours.

The Question underneath the Question#

If this sequence of posts has been asking: what cannot be optimized? — then mortality is the answer that makes all the other answers legible.

If the sequence has been asking: what is the internal audience actually afraid of? — then mortality is the thing underneath the attachment anxiety, underneath the belonging hunger, underneath the performance of self that never stops.

The optimizer is afraid of death. Not the concept — the fact. The actual nervous-system-level terror of stopping. And every optimization is, at some depth, an argument with that fact: if I just improve enough, maintain enough, become enough, the stopping will either not happen or will be survivable in some upgraded form.

It won’t. You won’t be upgraded past the ending.

What remains — the stillness, the friction, the interdependence, the body that just is — is not a practice for living well. It’s just the honest recognition that you’re already in the time you have, and there’s nothing to optimize about that, and you can’t get more of it by being better at it.

The optimizer wants to live forever. The honest person notices they are already dying, and finds — not peace, exactly — but something more useful than peace: the willingness to be in the time that’s left without trying to extend it.

That is not a technique. I could be wrong.
