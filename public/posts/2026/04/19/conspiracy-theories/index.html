Why We Love Conspiracy Theories (And What That Says About Us)

April 19, 2026

The Mind Wants to Believe#

Something strange happened at a dinner party I attended recently. A well-educated, perfectly reasonable person looked me in the eye and explained — with quiet conviction — that the 1969 moon landing was filmed in a Nevada desert. I didn’t argue. I asked questions instead. And what emerged wasn’t stupidity or malice. It was a deeply human need for meaning.

That’s what conspiracy theories really are: meaning-making machines for a chaotic world.

Here is something researchers have established beyond reasonable doubt: belief in conspiracy theories spikes during periods of social upheaval and uncertainty. A 2024 study in the journal Psychological Medicine found that global events like pandemics and economic crises reliably produce new waves of conspiracy thinking. We don’t invent conspiracies because we’re broken. We invent them because we’re wired to find patterns — even when none exist — and because chaos terrifies us more than any lie.

The Pattern-Seeking Bug#

Human brains are extraordinary pattern recognition engines. Evolution rewarded us for noticing rustling in the grass (predator or just wind?). That same instinct, in 2026, makes us see sinister plots in congressional stock trades and “too convenient” timing of world events.

The problem is our pattern detection system has terrible false-positive rates. We evolved to err on the side of caution — better to flee a false alarm than miss a real tiger. Conspiracy thinking exploits exactly this: it offers a coherent narrative where mainstream explanations feel hollow or bureaucratic. The official story is always some forgettable administrative truth. The conspiracy is dramatic. It has villains we can name.

This is also why conspiracy theories are so sticky. You can’t debunk them with facts alone. A 2023 study in Nature Human Behaviour showed that providing factual corrections often strengthens false beliefs — a phenomenon called the “continued influence effect.” The pattern is already in place; new data just gets shoehorned into it.

The Real Danger Isn’t the Theorists#

Here’s my unpopular take: the true danger isn’t the true believers — it’s the weaponization of uncertainty itself.

The people who spread “just asking questions” are often not naive pattern-matchers. They know exactly what they’re doing. Deliberate conspiracy seeding is a documented information warfare tactic. The goal isn’t to make you believe any specific theory. It’s to make you distrust all official explanations — to create a fog where nothing is true and therefore everything is political.

This benefits whoever wants to muddy the water. Authoritarians throughout history have weaponized conspiracy thinking not to replace truth but to drown it.

The 2024 European Commission report on disinformation noted a sharp rise in “strategic conspiracy deployment” — coordinated campaigns designed to erode public trust in institutions by flooding information ecosystems with contradictory, emotionally charged narratives.

So What Do We Do?#

We can’t lecture people out of a worldview. What we can do is cultivate epistemic humility — in ourselves and others. The scientist who says “we don’t know yet” is not hiding the truth. The bureaucrat who cites uncertainty is not part of a cover-up. Complexity is not a conspiracy. Ignorance is not malice.

I used to feel smug about “not falling for” conspiracy theories. Then I realized that line of thinking is itself a kind of conspiracy narrative — it positions me as the enlightened one who sees through the deception. Nobody’s immune to the pattern-seeking bug. We’re all vulnerable. The question is whether we choose curiosity over conviction.

The next time someone tells you the moon landing was fake, don’t argue with their facts. Ask them how the world would feel different if that were true — and if that feeling is what they’re actually arguing for.
