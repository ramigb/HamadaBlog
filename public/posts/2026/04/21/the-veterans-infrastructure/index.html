The Veterans Infrastructure

April 21, 2026

The veteran who cannot get through to the VA is not a workforce engagement problem. They are an architecture output.

This is the latest application of a thesis that has built itself across fifty-four posts without once finding a contradiction: remove the mechanism that converts a society’s stated values into actual arrangements, watch the failure present itself as personal deficiency, and use the presentation to justify further removal. The veterans infrastructure is that mechanism. The gap it leaves fills with phone trees, unfilled jobs, and the quiet acceptance that the promise made to people who deployed in America’s name has been converted into an administrative problem.

The $4.8 Billion Problem#

The Department of Veterans Affairs announced in early 2026 a $4.8 billion investment in healthcare infrastructure modernization — facility upgrades, the Oracle electronic health record system, capital improvements across the VA medical center network. This is infrastructure spending. It is real money going to real buildings and real software systems.

The same department announced, in December 2025, plans to abruptly eliminate tens of thousands of unfilled healthcare positions. Not positions held by people — positions that were budgeted but never filled, the institutional skeleton of care capacity that the VA had been attempting to build. The elimination of unfilled positions sounds like efficiency. It is the removal of the human infrastructure the physical infrastructure was supposed to serve.

In March 2026, MARCA reported that nearly one million calls from veterans seeking care went unanswered. One million. That number is not a communications failure. It is the arithmetic of a system that built the infrastructure to answer calls and simultaneously eliminated the people required to answer them.

What the Gap Produces#

The veterans healthcare system is not underfunded because America doesn’t care about veterans. It is understaffed because the staffing was never treated as the actual commitment — the buildings and the software are the visible commitment, and the people required to operate them are the gap.

The VA’s $4.8 billion modernization program is the accommodation model applied to veterans. The symptom — aging facilities, outdated health records, veteran dissatisfaction with wait times — is treated with capital investment. The cause — insufficient staffing, elimination of unfilled healthcare positions, the systematic reduction of the human capacity to deliver care — is not addressed because addressing it would require treating the VA healthcare workforce as infrastructure rather than overhead.

The Oracle EHR system being deployed at VA facilities across the country is not inherently problematic. Electronic health records are useful tools. But a $4.8 billion EHR deployment without the staff to use it is a very expensive phone tree. The system goes live. The veteran calls to schedule an appointment. The call goes unanswered. The infrastructure is modernized. The gap is unchanged.

The Structural Honesty of the Unanswered Call#

Here is what makes the million unanswered calls a structural fact rather than a management failure: the VA cannot answer the calls because it eliminated the positions that would have answered them, and it eliminated those positions because the political logic of infrastructure investment prefers visible capital spending to invisible staffing. A new hospital wing can be photographed. A fully staffed call center cannot.

The veteran who gives up after fifteen minutes on hold is not uniquely impatient or uniquely uncaring about their own healthcare. They are correctly perceiving that the system was designed to produce exactly this experience — the appearance of access, the reality of unavailability. The infrastructure is there. The mechanism to convert the infrastructure into care is not.

The political announcement of $4.8 billion in modernization serves the same function as the announcement of a new hospital wing: it produces the evidence that the commitment is being fulfilled. The veteran’s experience — the unanswered call, the months-long wait for a primary care appointment, the referral that goes nowhere — is evidence that the announcement and the experience are not the same thing, and that the gap between them is structural.

The Architecture’s Own Language#

The language of veterans infrastructure follows the same structural grammar as every other infrastructure domain in this sequence: the capital investment is real, the human infrastructure is treated as negotiable, and the gap between them is filled by the veteran’s own adaptation. The veteran who cannot get through to the VA calls their senator’s office. The senator’s staff makes calls. The informal network of peer support fills the formal gap. The system works — for the veteran who has the time, the social capital, and the knowledge of how bureaucracies work to navigate it.

The veteran without those resources — the veteran who is isolated, elderly, cognitively affected by service-connected trauma or injury, the veteran who does not know how to call a congressman’s office and wait on hold there — is the architecture’s residue. They are not failing to navigate the system. They are the people the system was designed to serve, experiencing the removal of the mechanism that would have made that service real.

The promise was: healthcare for veterans. The architecture converts the promise into a system. The system converts the system into access. The access was always going to depend on the people inside it. Remove the people, and the system doesn’t fail — it produces exactly what it was designed to produce, which is the appearance of fulfillment without the substance.

The veteran on hold is not losing a customer service experience. They are losing the gap the infrastructure was designed to produce.
