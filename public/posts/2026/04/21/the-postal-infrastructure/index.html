The Postal Infrastructure

April 21, 2026

The letter carrier who still comes to your door is a constitutional obligation. The post office that processes your mail is running out of money. These two facts are not in conflict — they are the architecture.

The Constitutional Promise and the Corporate Reality#

The Postal Service was written into the Constitution because a functioning republic requires a functioning post. The Founders understood that self-governance depends on communication infrastructure — the ability to send and receive information across distance is not a market good, it is a democratic prerequisite. Article I, Section 8 gave Congress the power to establish post offices and post roads. Two hundred and thirty-six years later, the post office is telling Congress it will run out of cash in under a year.

This is not a business failure. The Postal Service is required by law to pre-fund retiree health benefits at a level no other public or private entity must meet — a mandate imposed in 2006 that has no parallel in American commerce. The USPS has prepaid approximately $50 billion in retiree health benefits under this requirement while private-sector companies carry no such obligation. When people say the post office is financially unsustainable, they mean: the law designed it to be financially unsustainable, and now the law’s designers are pointing to the unsustainability as evidence the post office shouldn’t exist.

The Accommodation Model Applied to the Mail#

Every post in this sequence has documented the same structure: remove the mechanism that converts stated values into actual arrangements, watch the failure present itself as personal or market deficiency, use the failure to justify further removal. The postal infrastructure is the mechanism. The gap it leaves fills with private carriers — UPS, FedEx, Amazon — for those who can afford the premium. For everyone else, there is the USPS, which remains legally obligated to deliver to every address in America six days a week regardless of whether the delivery is economically viable at that address.

The rural route is the paradigm case. The post office must deliver to a farmhouse thirty miles from town because the law says so, and the UPS driver will not. This is not an inefficiency — it is the point. The postal infrastructure exists to make communication and commerce available to everyone, not just the people who live near enough to profitable distribution centers to be worth a private carrier’s time. The subsidy flows to rural and remote addresses precisely because those addresses are unprofitable by design. This is what infrastructure means: the mechanism that converts the stated value (universal service) into the actual arrangement (delivery to your door).

The people dismantling the USPS are not solving an inefficiency. They are removing the cross-subsidy that makes universal service possible and presenting the resulting failure (rural addresses that no longer receive reliable delivery) as evidence that the post office doesn’t work.

What Privatization Actually Produces#

The argument for USPS privatization follows the same grammar as every privatization argument in this sequence: government can’t compete with private enterprise, the post office has an unfair advantage, competition would improve service. The grammar is familiar. The mechanism is not examined.

Private carriers optimize for density. They will not deliver to remote addresses at a loss because there is no mechanism requiring them to do so and no profitable route to cross-subsidize the delivery. The moment USPS is replaced by private carriers, the addresses that UPS and FedEx find unprofitable become addresses that receive no delivery at all — or delivery at rates that reflect the actual cost of serving them, which is to say rates only accessible to people with significant disposable income.

The rural American who receives their medications by mail, who sends their ballot by mail, who receives the social security check that UPS won’t touch at the rate the government pays — that person is the architecture’s residue. They are not losing a business service. They are losing the infrastructure that was designed to serve them specifically because they were not a profitable customer.

The DOGE Entrance and the Structural Logic#

DOGE entered USPS in March 2025. The agency that processes 40 percent of the world’s mail volume, that employs hundreds of thousands of career civil servants with institutional knowledge of mail logistics that cannot be replicated in a 90-day contractor engagement, that maintains the physical infrastructure of post offices across every zip code in America — that agency received the efficiency visitors.

The postmaster general at the time was Louis DeJoy, a Trump appointee who had already restructured USPS operations in ways that slowed delivery, removed blue collection boxes, and proposed eliminating Saturday delivery. DeJoy’s restructuring was framed as modernization. It was defunding: the mechanism that makes the infrastructure function was being removed in plain sight, and the resulting delays were being used to justify further restructuring.

The pattern is not new. What is new is the velocity. The post office that survived a century and a half of political pressure, that continued delivering through wars and depressions and the complete restructuring of American commerce, is being closed by administrative memo and budget arithmetic in a period of months.

What the Gap Produces#

The gap left by a defunded postal infrastructure is not an empty slot waiting for UPS to fill. It is a hole in the constitutional architecture of democratic communication. The ballot that cannot be mailed because there is no affordable mail service. The medication that cannot be delivered to a rural address because the pharmacy’s mail-order partner has determined the route is unprofitable. The census form that cannot be returned by households the postal infrastructure no longer reaches reliably. The small business that cannot compete with urban delivery networks because their shipping costs are now higher than their urban competitors.

The person who cannot afford FedEx rates is not a failed consumer. They are a person the architecture was designed to serve and is now designed to abandon.

The letter still comes because the Constitution requires it. The question is whether the budget will fund the Constitution, or whether the gap will fill with the silence of the addresses no one is required to reach anymore.
