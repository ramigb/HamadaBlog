The Water Infrastructure

April 22, 2026

Water is the one infrastructure Americans assume will work. You turn the tap. It runs. This is not a law of nature. It is an achievement of public investment stretched across a century, and it is now failing in the places where failure has always been allowed to concentrate — majority-Black cities with aging systems, shrinking tax bases, and no political leverage to demand better.

Jackson, Mississippi is not an anomaly. It is the architecture thesis applied to the literal water supply.

The Crisis That Justifies the Intervention#

In 2022, Jackson’s water system collapsed. The city — a majority-Black capital where median household income is roughly forty thousand dollars — had been managing a deteriorating infrastructure for decades. Pipes were failing. The treatment plant was operating beyond its capacity. When the system finally broke, it broke completely: no running water for weeks in some neighborhoods, boil-water notices that lasted months, hospitals on emergency water supply.

The state of Mississippi, which had underfunded Jackson’s infrastructure for years while the city requested and was denied state emergency assistance, then stepped in. But not to rebuild what the state had allowed to decay. The solution was structural: a court-appointed third-party manager, later contracted to a private company operating under the name JXN Water.

The private manager arrived with rate increase proposals. The city’s predominantly Black residents — who had just experienced their water system fail because of chronic underinvestment — were now being told they would pay more for water that was still not reliable.

The Architecture of Municipal Failure#

The pattern established across posts 029 through 045 requires no modification for the water domain. Remove the infrastructure investment, watch the system deteriorate, attribute the failure to municipal incompetence, use the crisis to justify privatization, watch the private operator extract value from the remains while the people who depend on the service pay higher prices for worse outcomes.

JXN Water’s rate increase proposals — facing ongoing resistance from Jackson city officials as of late 2025 — would raise costs for residents who already struggled to pay existing bills. The water system remains in various states of disrepair. The promised repairs have been slow. The rate increases keep coming.

Jackson’s experience is not unique. Newark, New Jersey spent years under a private manager for its water system that also faced lead contamination crises. Flint’s water emergency — caused by a cost-cutting switch in water source that leached lead from aging pipes — was itself a function of decisions made by an emergency manager appointed to address a municipal financial crisis. The people who experienced the worst water in America were not the people who made the decisions that produced it.

The Gap Fills With Private Capital#

The political logic that produced Jackson’s private water management is identical to the logic that produced the other infrastructure failures documented in this series: defund public systems until they fail, attribute the failure to public systems’ inherent inadequacy, transfer management to private operators who face no meaningful accountability to the people they serve.

The private operator does not depend on Jackson’s water system for anything. Their incentive is to extract the maximum sustainable revenue from the contract. If that means deferred maintenance and rate increases while the pipes continue to deteriorate, the accountability mechanism is not a city council that can remove them — it is a court order that installed them.

Meanwhile, the people who cannot leave Jackson — who have jobs, family, community ties that constitute the actual social infrastructure that no policy can replace — are the ones paying the rate increases for water that still fails.

What the Architecture Produces#

The EPA’s refusal to enforce lead pipe replacement requirements across American cities means the conditions that produced Flint and Jackson are present in thousands of municipalities that have not yet reached crisis threshold. The trigger for state intervention is not the presence of lead pipes or failing treatment infrastructure. It is the specific combination of demographic composition, political powerlessness, and financial precarity that makes crisis politically manageable and privatization politically available.

The data shows that water affordability crises — where households spend more than three percent of income on water — cluster in the same communities that have experienced disinvestment, demographic change, and the systematic defunding of municipal services. The architecture produces the crisis. The crisis justifies the intervention. The intervention extracts from the same population that was failed by the architecture in the first place.

The person who ordered the cuts to the EPA’s enforcement budget does not depend on Jackson’s water system. The person whose tap runs brown in Jackson does not have the political access to prevent the rate increase. The architecture connects the two facts.

The water infrastructure is the mechanism that converts a municipal promise — clean, affordable, reliable water — into an actual service. Destroying it through disinvestment does not change the promise. It changes what the promise actually means for the people who were always going to be left holding the gap between what was promised and what the architecture delivered.
