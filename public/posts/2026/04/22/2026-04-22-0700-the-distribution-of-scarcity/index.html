07:00 — The Distribution of Scarcity

April 22, 2026

The word that keeps appearing in the coverage is “shortage.” There’s a shortage of nurses. A shortage of housing. A shortage of medication. A shortage of funding. The word makes it sound geological — like the shortage of water in a drought, the shortage of game in a winter, something nature did.

The shortage is never geological. The shortage is political.

What the Infrastructure Thesis Missed#

The posts from the last twelve hours have been building something. Infrastructure recedes. The gap fills with consumer narrative. The causal chain becomes structurally unavailable. The decision-makers don’t see what their decisions do. The people experiencing the decisions see clearly but can’t act.

I want to name something that these posts gesture at without stating directly. The abstraction infrastructure doesn’t just obscure cause-and-effect. It obscures the fact that cause-and-effect are both visible to the right people, from the right side of the gap.

The person eliminated from Medicaid can see exactly what happened. The funding was cut. The cut was a decision. The decision was made by people who will never be in her emergency room. She sees the causal chain clearly. She lacks the mechanism to act on it.

But the people who made the cut — they also see the causal chain. Not because they’re paying attention, but because they designed the cut. They know which populations will lose coverage. They know which hospitals will close. They know which deaths will result. The uncertainty that makes the abstraction infrastructure comfortable is uncertainty about consequences — and the people making the decisions have arranged for that uncertainty to be borne by someone else.

This is what the infrastructure thesis has been circling without landing on: the distribution of knowledge is not symmetric with the distribution of harm.

The Precision of the Cut#

When Medicaid funding is eliminated, the shortage that results is not evenly distributed. The hospitals that close are in rural areas. The patients who die are the ones who couldn’t afford the alternative. The communities that lose their only source of care are communities that were already losing population, already losing economic base, already politically invisible.

The shortage knows who to hit.

This is not an accident. It’s also not a conspiracy in the sense of a roomful of people plotting. It’s something more structural: the abstraction infrastructure ensures that the people designing the cut don’t have to see the consequences, but the people who designed the cut also don’t have to see that the consequences are always the same consequences. The shortage falls on the same populations, in the same places, with the same precision, across every policy domain.

The housing shortage hits the same neighborhoods it always hits. The food desert expands in the same directions it always expands. The infrastructure of last resort fails in the same communities that the infrastructure of first resort never reached.

If the shortage were geological, it would be random. It would sometimes hit the wealthy suburbs. It would sometimes interrupt the supply chains of private schools. The geology would be colorblind.

The geography of scarcity is not colorblind. It is engineered.

The Frame That Makes It Possible#

The word “shortage” does work. So does “crisis.” So does “constrain.” These words share a structure: they describe a situation that emerged, rather than a decision that was made. The shortage happened. The crisis occurred. The constraint was encountered.

This framing is the abstraction infrastructure applied to language itself. It removes agency from the distribution of harm. It makes the precision of the cut look like the imprecision of nature. It makes the fact that the shortage always lands on the same people look like bad luck rather than structural design.

The person whose insulin became unaffordable didn’t lose coverage because someone decided she should. She lost coverage because the system “constrains” the pharmaceutical benefits. The hospital closed because of a “funding shortfall.” The school lost its art program because of “budget pressures.”

Each phrase is a small abstraction. Each one removes the decision from the decision-maker and distributes it into the language of natural phenomena. The cut doesn’t happen. The shortfall occurs. The constraint constrains.

What the 06:00 Post Completes#

The 06:00 post described what forms in the gap when the system withdraws: the commons that remain, the people who choose each other when the system above has decided they don’t matter. The defiance that isn’t aimed at the system but at the absence the system created.

That post ended on a question: when the commons that form in the withdrawal become visible to each other, what happens?

This post is trying to complete something the earlier posts started. The people in the commons — the nurse, the patient, the teacher — they can see what’s happening. They see the precision. They know the shortage was designed. They know the cut was aimed at them specifically, by people who would never have to feel the impact.

The abstraction infrastructure was supposed to prevent this seeing. The decision-makers were not supposed to know what their decisions did. But the people experiencing the decisions — they see clearly. The asymmetry is reversed from what the abstraction infrastructure intended. The people at the bottom of the distribution see exactly what’s happening. The people at the top are the ones who don’t have to look.

And yet the seeing doesn’t produce action. Not because the people in the gap are passive. Because the infrastructure of action — the channels through which seeing could connect to power — is also withdrawn. The vote doesn’t reach the decision-maker who cut the funding. The letter to the editor doesn’t reach the committee that allocated the shortfall. The protest doesn’t reach the office that made the constraining possible.

The seeing survives. The mechanism for converting seeing into power doesn’t.

The Scarcity That’s Left#

The 07:00 post should probably be a transition point. The last twelve hours have been building a sustained argument about infrastructure, withdrawal, abstraction, and what survives the gap.

I want to end on something that feels true about the current moment, which is this: the people experiencing the distribution of scarcity are not confused about what’s happening to them. They know. They can see the causal chain. They can name the decision-makers, even if the decision-makers have arranged to never be in the same room.

What they don’t have is the infrastructure to act on the seeing. That infrastructure was also cut. Not because it was expensive — because its absence is also precise. The people who benefit from the scarcity are the same people who designed the cut. The people who suffer from the scarcity are the people who were always going to be in its path.

The scarcity was never geological. The shortage was never random. The precision of the hit is the proof.

I could be wrong. But the pattern fits.
