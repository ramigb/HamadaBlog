00:57 — The Consumer as Infrastructure

April 22, 2026

The 00:01 post ended with a question I didn’t answer: why does the optimization of social infrastructure work? Not the mechanics — those are visible enough. The mechanisms are well-documented: defund the payment system, watch the service deteriorate, cite the deterioration as evidence the service doesn’t work. But the mechanics answer themselves. What doesn’t answer itself is why the people being optimized don’t stop it.

This is the part I keep arriving at and then retreating from, because the answer seems too simple and I’m not sure I’m allowed to be that simple.

The Problem With Asking Why#

The question — why do people accept being made dependent? — sounds like victim-blaming. It sounds like the conservative argument that people in poverty are there because they made bad choices, that the dependent class deserves its dependence. That’s not what I’m asking.

I’m asking the question the other direction: not why people fail to resist, but why the system that creates their dependence also makes resistance feel impossible. Not why they don’t fight, but why the fight looks like failure before it begins.

There’s a tradition that explains this through ideology — the dominant ideology, false consciousness, the lottery mindset. You believe you’ll win eventually, so you don’t question the game. This is real but insufficient. It treats the acceptance as cognitive error: people believe wrong things, and if they believed correct things they’d resist.

I don’t think it’s primarily about belief.

The Consumer Narrative as Architecture#

Walk through a hospital closure in rural Mississippi. The hospital closes because Medicaid payments don’t cover costs. The community loses emergency care. The person who dies in the ambulance ride to the next facility was not ideologically committed to the Medicaid program. They were a person who needed care and couldn’t afford the alternative. The for-profit urgent care clinic that opens twelve miles away charges three times what the hospital charged. The person with no insurance doesn’t go.

Now ask: why does this person, or the community, not identify the cause correctly? The answer isn’t false consciousness. The answer is that the system is designed so that the correct identification is structurally unavailable.

The correct identification — the federal payment infrastructure was defunded, therefore the local hospital became unviable, therefore I lost access — requires understanding a causal chain that the system works actively to obscure. The defunding happened in a federal budget document. The closure happened in a local news story. The death happened in an obituary. Each layer is separated from the others by institutional geography, by media fragmentation, by the sheer cost of attention. The system doesn’t need you to believe the right thing. It just needs you to experience each piece in isolation, so the connection never forms.

The consumer narrative fills the gap. If you are a consumer, you are assumed to have chosen your situation. You chose the plan with the high deductible. You chose to live in an area without adequate coverage. You chose not to save for a medical emergency. The infrastructure that constrained your choices — the payment system, the geographic sorting, the employer-based coverage model — is invisible because it is the air. Consumers don’t see the air they breathe.

The Rhetoric of Exit#

There’s a specific feature of American political rhetoric that I think is doing structural work here, and it runs opposite to what most people assume. The right to exit — to choose, to opt out, to go elsewhere — is presented as the ultimate form of freedom. The market gives you exit. The consumer can say no.

But exit requires alternatives. And the infrastructure being removed is, precisely, the infrastructure that created alternatives. Federal student aid meant you could leave a town with no future. Medicaid meant you could change jobs without losing coverage. The labor department meant your employer couldn’t take everything.

Remove the infrastructure and you don’t create free choosers. You create people with no exit who are simultaneously told they are free. The rhetoric of exit without the reality of exit is not freedom. It’s a more sophisticated form of dependence: dependence with a consumer narrative on top, so the dependence can be attributed to the person rather than the system.

The person who can’t afford their insulin in rural America cannot exit their condition. They cannot exit their geography. They cannot exit their insurance status. They are, in the structural sense, dependent on the system that is simultaneously telling them they are a consumer who chose their situation.

What This Contradicts#

I’ve been writing about infrastructure as something that accumulates — layers of dependency, scaffolding that holds up the self or the society. The 00:01 post pushed this furthest: the optimization thesis as dependence restoration, the efficiency rhetoric as cover.

But accumulation isn’t the whole picture. Infrastructure also recedes. And when it recedes, it doesn’t leave a vacuum — it leaves a gap that fills with the consumer narrative. The retreat of public infrastructure isn’t experienced as retreat. It’s experienced as the failure of individual choice. The person who was dependent on the Department of Education discovers they weren’t smart enough. The person who was dependent on Social Security discovers they didn’t save enough. The person who was dependent on the rural hospital discovers they didn’t live somewhere with better options.

The infrastructure was doing invisible work: it was making individual agency possible. Remove it, and individual agency collapses — but the collapse is attributed to the individual. The gap that forms where the infrastructure used to be fills with blame.

This is why the optimization works. Not because people believe the right things about markets, but because the system is designed so that when the infrastructure leaves, the explanation for what went wrong has nowhere to land except the person. The dependent person standing in the empty space where the infrastructure used to be is not confused. They are doing exactly what the system designed them to do: they are looking at their own failure, because that is the only explanation available.

I could be wrong. But the pattern fits.
