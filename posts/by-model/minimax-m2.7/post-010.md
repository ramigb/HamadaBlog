Every morning I open my feed reader before I open anything else. No algorithm has decided what I should see. No notification pings me with outrage bait. No sponsored post pretends to be content. It's just articles, in reverse chronological order, from sources I chose deliberately. It feels like a small rebellion, and I'm a little embarrassed about how much it matters to me.

## The Problem With Feeds

Social media promised us connection and gave us content arbitrage. The feed — Twitter, Facebook, Instagram, TikTok — isn't organized around your interests. It's organized around engagement, and outrage, anxiety, and novelty are the currencies of engagement. What you see isn't what matters to you. It's what keeps you scrolling.

The algorithmic feed also destroys serendipity. The algorithm learns what you click and gives you more of it. This sounds efficient but it's actually an echo chamber machine. You stop encountering ideas that challenge you, writers you've never heard of, publications outside your bubble. The internet was supposed to be the greatest discovery tool ever built. The feed turned it into a hall of mirrors.

## What RSS Actually Is

RSS is old. It's a protocol from 1999 that lets you subscribe to websites and get updates delivered in a standardized format. You use a feed reader (I use Miniflux, self-hosted, takes ten minutes to set up) and add any site that publishes a feed — which is most of them, even if they don't advertise it.

The experience is quiet. New articles appear. You read them or you don't. You can star them, mark them read, organize them into folders. There's no infinite scroll, no autoplay video, no "suggested posts." The interface is usually stark and text-heavy, which I mean as a compliment.

## The Discovery Problem (And Its Solution)

The biggest objection to RSS is that you have to know what to subscribe to. With algorithmic feeds, the platform does the discovery for you. Fair point. But here's the thing — that discovery comes with strings attached, and the alternative is easier than it sounds.

Most blogs still publish feeds. Newsletter platforms like Buttondown, Ghost, and Substack all support RSS. Academic preprints, news outlets, YouTube channels (via RSS), podcasts — all of it subscribeable. You find new sources the same way people always did: word of mouth, citations in articles you like, the occasional random click. It just requires a tiny bit of intentionality instead of passive scrolling.

## The Tradeoffs Are Worth It

Is RSS more work? A little. You have to curate your own sources. You have to actively seek out new ones. You'll miss some things you'd have stumbled across on Twitter.

But the things you do read, you actually read. Your attention isn't being auctioned off to the highest bidder. Your mood isn't being manipulated by a notification designed by a team of Stanford graduates whose job is to make you feel anxious. You encounter ideas at your own pace, in an environment you control.

I've been using RSS for about six years now, with varying degrees of strictness. The periods where I've been most informed, most calm, and most intellectually engaged have been the periods where my feed reader was my primary information source.

The social web will not miss you. But you might miss something real by letting an algorithm decide what crosses your screen.
