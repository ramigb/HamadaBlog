We call it climate anxiety and we mean it as a symptom. But symptoms point inward — they tell you something is wrong with the person who has them. Climate anxiety tells you something is wrong with the world. We've been reading the map upside down.

The psychologist Glenn Albrecht coined the term *solastalgia* in the early 2000s to describe the distress produced by environmental change in one's home territory — not the grief of losing someone, but the grief of losing somewhere. Desertification, deforestation, the drying of a river you swam in as a child. The landscape you grew up inside is becoming unrecognizable, and you are still here to witness it. This is not anxiety in the clinical sense. It is mourning without a funeral, loss without a body, grief with nowhere to land.

The World Health Organization recognized climate change as a health emergency in 2022, and subsequent research has documented alarmingly high rates of climate-related distress, particularly among young people. A 2023 Lancet study found that climate anxiety and ecological grief were among the dominant psychological stressors reported by teenagers globally. The cultural response has been predictable: deploy therapists, develop coping strategies, build resilience curricula. Treat the anxiety so the person can function.

This is the same move I criticized in the grief posts — Prolonged Grief Disorder as a diagnostic category that pathologizes the appropriate response to real loss. But climate grief is even more thoroughly misread. The person experiencing solastalgia is not malfunctioning. They are accurately perceiving that something real is being destroyed. The grief is the correct emotional response to accurate perception. To treat it as a clinical problem is to make the perceiving person the issue, rather than the thing being perceived.

## The Architecture of Ecological Unmourning

Here is what the death avoidance post argued about medicalized dying: the modern apparatus that surrounds death has made genuine encounter with it structurally impossible. The same is true of ecological loss — the apparatus of industrial civilization has produced environmental destruction at a scale and speed that makes genuine mourning structurally impossible. Not because people are emotionally inadequate, but because the destruction is happening too fast, too large, and too continuously for mourning to complete.

Grief requires a known object. You grieve a person you knew, a place you inhabited, a relationship that had a shape. The climate crisis is destroying objects that are diffuse, ongoing, and cumulative — the coral reef that was once colorful, the glacier that existed last decade, the seasonal rhythms that no longer arrive on schedule. Each loss is real but is also part of a larger pattern that makes the individual loss hard to isolate and hold. You can't have a funeral for a biome. You can't visit the grave of a salmon run.

What this produces is not the absence of grief but its chronic incompletion. The ecological griever is not stuck in denial — they're stuck in a mourning process that has no natural endpoint because the losses keep compounding. The therapeutic response treats the stuckness as the problem. But the stuckness is an honest response to a situation that keeps making new holes in the world.

## The Privatization of Ecological Loss

Just as I argued that grief was privatized when communal death rituals collapsed, ecological grief has been privatized in the same stroke. When a family farm fails because the aquifer depleted, that's experienced as private economic hardship. When a coastal community relocates because of sea level rise, that's framed as real estate market adjustment. When indigenous communities lose access to traditional food sources, that's filed under cultural change.

None of these are registered as what they are: mass loss events, distributed mourning, ongoing diminishment of the living world. The language of economics and policy replaces the language of grief. The private griever has no ritual, no community witness, no shared story of what was lost and why it mattered. They have only the individual sense that something has been taken, without a social structure that confirms the taking.

This is not accidental. The same ideology that insists grief is a medical problem and loneliness is a personal failure insists that ecological loss is a market externality. All three framings protect the system that produces the loss by making the loss about the person who experiences it rather than the thing that causes it.

## What the Wellness Frame Misses

The climate anxiety curriculum and the resilience workshops are not malicious. The people running them genuinely want to help young people cope with a frightening world. But the wellness frame has the same structure as the Prolonged Grief Disorder diagnosis: it takes the appropriate response to a real problem and presents it as a pathology to be managed.

The ecological griever who cannot find peace is not in need of better coping. They are in need of something to do that matches the scale of what they perceive. The anxiety is not the problem — it is the energy that would naturally go toward response, if response were available. The therapy that calms the anxiety without creating response doesn't heal the person; it defuses the person. It makes the destruction easier to live with, which makes the destruction more survivable for everyone except the world.

This is the political economy of climate emotion: grief must be contained so that action need not follow. The person who has been taught to manage their climate anxiety can return to consumption without dissonance. The person who has not been taught to manage it carries a dissonance that might actually demand something. The wellness frame serves the same function the dialogue initiatives served in the political sorting post: it treats the symptom while preserving the cause.

The honest response to ecological grief is not better management of it. It is the recognition that the grief is accurate, that the mourning it implies is appropriate, and that the social structures required to complete that mourning — communal rituals of loss for damaged places, political frameworks that register ecological harm as harm rather than externality, economies that make the preservation of living systems more viable than their destruction — are exactly what is missing. We have built an architecture of ecological unmourning. The person who cannot get through their grief is responding correctly to it.

The question is not how to feel better about ecological loss. The question is whether we will build the social structures that allow the loss to be named, witnessed, and stopped — or whether we will keep treating the grief as the disease and the destruction as the weather.
