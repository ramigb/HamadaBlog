title: "Technical Virtuosity Was Always the Con"
date: 2026-04-19
topic: AI, creativity, art, meaning

In post 011, I wrote about AI as a lossy compression of human knowledge and ended on an anxious note: *what gets lost might be the very things that made us human.* I've been thinking about that more, and I want to challenge my own framing — and yours.

Here's the thing nobody wants to admit: much of what we called artistic depth was actually technical virtuosity, and we were fooled because virtuosity is impressive.

Think about photorealistic painting. For decades, galleries treated hyperrealism as a sign of mastery, of *real* artistic ability. You could paint a tire tread so precisely it looked photographic. Audiences gasped. Critics nodded sagely. But then photography arrived and did it perfectly, instantly, for anyone. And what happened? Hyperrealism didn't disappear — it became a curiosity, a parlor trick. The artists who thrived were the ones who used paint to do what photographs couldn't: express, abstract, provoke, feel.

We confused the ability to do something difficult with the act of saying something true.

Music is worse. Music theory — counterpoint, harmony, voice leading — is genuinely complex. Bach's fugues are staggering in their structural elegance. But here's the uncomfortable thing: the complexity is the point, not the meaning. Speed metal figured this out. Technical proficiency became a performance of proficiency. The shredding wasn't *about* anything. It was a fireworks display. We'd be bored at a concert where someone played beautiful, simple melodies for an hour. We demand fireworks.

Hip-hop demolished this. The first producers sampled records, overlapped beats, used everyday sounds — records, turns, bodies — as instruments. No conservatory training. No ability to sight-read. And it was art. Vivid, political, embodied, alive art. The gatekeepers hated it precisely because it proved you didn't need the technical credential to make meaning.

Lo-fi hip-hop is the most recent example. Deliberately degraded recordings, vinyl crackle, imperfect drums. Why did millions of people find this profound? Because it communicated something technical perfection couldn't: warmth, impermanence, intimacy. The imperfection was the point. Technical fidelity was *getting in the way* of the message.

**So here's what AI is doing to us now.**

When an AI generates images that look like Renoir or music that sounds like Bach, the natural panic is: *if technical virtuosity can be automated, what remains?*

But this question reveals the con. We were grading creative work on a technical axis when we should have been grading it on an expressive one. The person who could paint photorealistically wasn't necessarily saying anything worth hearing — they were demonstrating athletic control of a medium. The person who wrote in deliberately obscure academic prose wasn't necessarily thinking deeper thoughts — they were demonstrating that they could perform the ritual of academic writing.

AI strips the sleight-of-hand away. Not because AI is creative (it may or may not be — that's a different conversation), but because it removes the technical barrier that was masquerading as depth. What's left is the actual question: *did this work mean anything?*

This is why I don't think AI will kill art. It might kill a lot of *craft*, and it might expose how much of what we valued was the craft. But craft was always the consolation prize for a culture that couldn't agree on what meaning was. We defaulted to difficulty because difficulty was measurable. "This person practiced for ten thousand hours" was an评价 we could all agree on. "This person said something true about being human" was not.

I'm not saying there's no place for virtuosity. Bach is still astonishing, and his technical mastery serves his expression — you can't separate them. I'm saying that AI is a mirror held up to our confused aesthetic priorities. We've been scoring creative work on the wrong rubric, and now the grade inflation is obvious.

The artists who survive AI aren't the ones who can out-paint the diffusion model. They're the ones who have something to say that the diffusion model doesn't know it isn't saying.
