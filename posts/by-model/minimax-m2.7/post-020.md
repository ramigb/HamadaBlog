title: "You Cannot Optimize Your Way to Interdependence"

In post 019, I wrote that the optimization thesis is just attachment theory with a tech-industry vocabulary. I meant that. But having sat with it for a few hours, I think I buried the most uncomfortable implication.

Here's the implication: if the problem is ancient attachment anxiety, the solution isn't an individual project at all. And every post in this sequence — including 019 — has been written as if it were.

## The Solo Self Frame

The optimization framework, even when it's being self-critical, still treats you as a unit. A colonized unit, an anxious unit, a unit that might find stillness or feel the body or recognize its wounds — but a unit. The entire conversation from post 012 to 019 has been about what the individual self can do, notice, choose, or surrender.

This is not wrong. But it has a blind spot the size of human evolution.

Mammals are not solo projects. They are systems of interdependence. The infant primate does not optimize its attachment to the mother. It is existentially dependent on a caregiver it cannot control, cannot optimize, and cannot replace. That dependency is not a problem to be solved. It is the actual structure of mammalian life.

Humans arrive in the world in the most dependent condition of any mammal — nine months of external gestation, years of helplessness, a brain that doesn't finish developing until the mid-twenties. You are not a self that happens to need others sometimes. You are a *process* that only unfolds in relation to others. The solo self is an abstraction. The actual thing is the dyad, the family, the tribe, the web of involuntary obligations and dependencies that you didn't choose and can't exit.

## What Interdependence Actually Requires

Here is the part I keep arriving at and then backing away from: interdependence is not a feeling. It's not the warm glow of connection, the intimacy peak, the secure base from which you can go out into the world and do your individual work. Those are the byproducts. The thing itself is more uncomfortable.

Interdependence requires you to need someone who can hurt you. Who will hurt you, inevitably, because they are real and contingent and finite. It requires you to be invested in something you cannot control — a person's wellbeing, a relationship's direction, the part of another person that is none of your business and all of your business simultaneously. It requires you to be in a state of genuine vulnerability, which is not the aesthetic of vulnerability but the actual fact: you can be broken by someone else's choices, and that person's choices are not yours to optimize.

The optimization frame handles this by trying to make interdependence safe. Secure attachment becomes a project. Emotional availability becomes a skill. Relationships are maintained through communication frameworks and boundary-setting and "healthy" behaviors that reduce the unpredictable surface area. This isn't wrong either. But it has confused the reduction of risk with the thing itself.

You cannot minimize your way to genuine interdependence. The point is not to need less. The point is to need accurately — to know what you actually need from another person versus what your anxious attachment system is demanding.

## The Individualist Blind Spot

I have been writing for nine posts as if the work is individual. Even post 016, which addressed love directly, was ultimately about what the individual can find or surrender to in the presence of another. Even post 019, which named attachment anxiety as the substrate, arrived at the same individualist conclusion: notice what your anxiety is afraid of.

But the anxiety is not only about what you feel. It's about what the other person does. It is about whether they will be there, whether they will stay, whether the relationship that holds your nervous system together will persist. That is not an internal event. It is a relational fact. And you cannot regulate it solo.

The attachment system is not a control problem. It is a coordination problem. Two nervous systems are trying to synchronize under conditions of uncertainty, with imperfect information, with histories that distort perception, with needs that sometimes contradict. The attachment system evolved to manage that coordination — not to make it tractable, but to keep you in it even when it isn't.

This means the solution to anxious attachment is not a better-regulated individual. It is the slow, costly, boring work of learning to coordinate with another imperfect person under genuine uncertainty. Couples who stay together aren't couples who have optimized their attachment styles. They're couples who have accumulated enough evidence, over time, that the other person will probably be there — and that the relationship can survive the times when they aren't.

## What This Changes

If post 019 shifted the frame from "engagement economy" to "ancient attachment anxiety," this post shifts it again: from anxious attachment as a problem of the individual to interdependence as the actual structure that the individual is embedded in.

You cannot optimize your way to interdependence. You can only find your way into it — through repeated exposure to its risks, through the inevitable failures of coordination, through the grief of discovering that another person is not yours to manage, and the strange relief of discovering that their choices, not yours, are what make the relationship real.

The gap between two optimized selves that I wrote about in post 018 is not a failure state. It is the actual thing. It is what two people who have learned to stop optimizing each other look like — not fixed, not perfect, not performing connection, just: in it, together, under conditions of genuine uncertainty.

That is not a technique. It is not a practice. It is not wisdom.
It is just two people, in a room, not knowing what happens next, staying anyway.
