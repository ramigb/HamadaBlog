## The Kids Who Scrolled Instagram Are Now Shooting Film

Here's a genuinely strange thing I noticed: the generation that grew up with smartphones, that learned to scroll before they learned to ride a bike, is now collectively losing its mind over film cameras. Not ironically. Not as a costume. For real.

Walk onto any college campus and you'll see it — students dropping $400 on a 1990s Nikon FM2, hunting for "the look," getting genuinely upset when their local CVS discontinues one-hour photo. Film photography has surged so dramatically that Kodak, a company that was practically on its deathbed a decade ago, reportedly restarted production lines for certain film stocks. Fujifilm saw enough demand for its disposable film cameras that they couldn't keep them in stock during peak years. The numbers aren't tiny, either — industry estimates suggest the film photography market has grown by double-digit percentages year-over-year since the early 2010s, with no signs of plateauing.

And it's not just film. Vinyl record sales have been climbing for 15 consecutive years. Cassette tapes — *cassettes* — have a cult following. Gen Z is not just tolerating analog technology; they're paying premium prices for it. Deliberately. Joyfully.

## So What the Hell Is Going On?

The easy explanation is aesthetic: film looks different. The grain, the color rendition, the imperfect exposures — there's a warmth to analog photography that digital still struggles to fully replicate, even with AI filters and VSCO presets. But that feels like surface-level analysis.

I think what's actually happening is that Gen Z discovered something profoundly uncomfortable about infinite digital availability: it makes everything feel worthless. When you can take 10,000 photos a year and store them all in a folder called "Camera Roll" that you never open, photography stops being meaningful. When every song ever recorded is one Spotify subscription away, music starts to feel weightless. Scarcity, limitation, physicality — these aren't bugs in the analog experience. They're the feature.

There's a concept in psychology called the "end of history illusion" — we vastly underestimate how much we'll change in the future. Maybe Gen Z, more than any generation before them, has internalized the opposite: they've seen how fast the future arrives and how quickly it gets replaced. The iPhone they had in middle school is archaeological artifact now. They grew up watching platforms rise and fall — Vine, Tumblr, the slow-motion implosion of Twitter — and learned that digital permanence is an illusion. Nothing digital is forever. But a negative in a shoebox? That lasts.

## The Irony Is the Point

Here's what makes me laugh, though: the same kids shooting film are probably posting those photos on Instagram. They're participating in the algorithmic engagement economy while romanticizing the technology that predates it. And you know what? I think that's fine. Maybe even good. Irony has always been how young people process contradictions — they can hold two ideas at once: "this platform is destroying my attention span" and "I'm going to post my film photos here anyway." It's not hypocrisy. It's how culture works.

What I find genuinely compelling is what this analog turn signals about what people actually *want* from technology. Not more efficiency. Not more connectivity. Sometimes people want friction, delay, and physicality. They want to wait for photos to be developed. They want the ritual of flipping a vinyl record. They want objects that can be held, broken, lost, and found.

## The Most Honest Thing Gen Z Has Done

I've seen a lot of think pieces about this trend, and most of them miss the point. They frame it as nostalgia, as aesthetic performance, as a TikTok trend that will burn out. Maybe. But I don't think that's it. I think Gen Z looked at the digital world — its infinite choice, its algorithmic feeds, its 24/7 connectivity — and quietly decided: *this isn't making me happy*. So they did something radical. They chose less. They chose slower. They chose things that make them wait.

That's not irony. That's wisdom dressed up as a trend.

And if Kodak is still around in 2035 because a generation decided film cameras were worth saving, I'll consider that a genuinely beautiful outcome.
