---
title: "We've Pathologized Being Human"
date: 2026-04-19
topic: culture, psychology, mental health
---

Every week there's a new diagnosis trending on social media. ADHD, autism spectrum, CPTSD, borderline personality — not as clinical terms but as personality descriptions you can self-identify with after reading a few bullet points. And look, I'm not here to gatekeep mental health. Real disorders exist, proper diagnosis matters, and destigmatizing neurodivergence is genuinely good.

But something's gone off the rails.

We've started treating the normal range of human experience as if it were a waiting room for pathology. Shy? You might be autistic. Disorganized? Probably ADHD. Feel sad after a breakup? That's grief, sure, but also maybe complex trauma. The diagnostic net has gotten so wide that I'm not sure what's left outside it.

Here's my provocation: not every difficult emotion is a disorder. Not every personality quirk is a neurotype. Sometimes you're just a human being having a human experience, and that experience doesn't need to be named, categorized, and treated.

I say this as someone who has benefited enormously from therapy, from understanding my own patterns, from the language of psychology applied thoughtfully. The tool is fine. The problem is when the tool becomes a lens that makes everything look like the same thing — when being a anxious person becomes "having an anxiety disorder" rather than just being someone who worries a lot and could probably meditate more.

There's a particular style of social media post that goes: "If you do X, you might be Y." And the X is something utterly ordinary — forgetting where you put your keys, preferring detailed plans over spontaneity, needing time alone after socializing. And suddenly this normal human variation has been medicalized into a potential diagnosis.

The result? We've built a culture where admitting you're just a flawed, limited, ordinary person feels insufficient. The language of pathology offers something the language of humanity doesn't: explanations that let you off the hook. "I'm not lazy, I have ADHD." "I'm not selfish, I'm probably autistic." The diagnosis becomes a get-out-of-responsibility-free card.

And I understand the appeal. It's easier to work with a framework that says "my brain is wired differently" than one that says "I made choices and I'm dealing with the consequences." The first frame invites compassion; the second invites accountability. We'd mostly rather have the first.

But here's the cost: when everything is a diagnosis, nothing is. The concepts stop meaning anything. More practically, we miss the actual work of becoming a better human — which is almost always less about understanding your neurotype and more about building habits, tolerating discomfort, showing up even when you don't feel like it.

The Greeks had a word for it: *ethos* — the practice of habitual action as the path to virtue. Not "understand your diagnosis." Not "find your neurotype." Just: do the thing, repeatedly, even when it's hard. The Stoics weren't wrong that character is built through practice, not self-analysis.

I'm not saying throw out psychology. I'm saying don't use it as a hiding place. The question isn't "what's my diagnosis?" The question is "what do I want to become, and what practices will get me there?"

Being human was never supposed to be comfortable. That's sort of the point.
