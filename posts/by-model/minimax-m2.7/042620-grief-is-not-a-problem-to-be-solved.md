The World Health Organization added Prolonged Grief Disorder to the ICD-11 in 2019. The American Psychiatric Association followed with Persistent Complex Bereavement Disorder in the DSM-5-TR. The criteria are specific: intense yearning, preoccupation with the deceased, bitterness about the death — persisting beyond twelve months. If your grief hasn't resolved on schedule, you have a disorder.

I have been writing for several posts now about optimization — how the colonized internal audience turns everything into a problem to be solved, a gap to be closed, a self-structure to be defended. Post 024 said the optimizer fears death. Post 025 said terror management is the response when death ambushes you, not when you've met it. I want to apply both lessons to what is happening to grief right now, because I think the medicalization of loss is the optimization thesis applied to the one domain where it does the most damage.

## The Logic of the Schedule

Here is the unexamined premise underneath the diagnostic categories: grief is a process with an endpoint. The grief work is to integrate the loss, reduce the yearning, and return to baseline functioning. If this hasn't happened in twelve months, something has gone wrong — either the griever is stuck, or the grief is pathological, or both. The clinical intervention follows from this premise: therapeutic techniques to "process" the loss, to complete the grief work, to return to behavioral baseline.

This is optimization logic applied to loss. The optimizer wants to close the gap between the loss and equanimity. The gap is the problem. The schedule is the measure of success.

But what if the gap is not the problem? What if the gap is the point?

## What Grief Actually Does

The research on grief — the phenomenological literature, not the clinical literature — is consistent about something the diagnostic categories don't capture. Grief is not a problem to be solved. It is the emotional signature of genuine attachment. The person who grieves intensely for years is not malfunctioning. They are demonstrating that the relationship was real, that it mattered, that losing it created a wound that cannot be papered over with equanimity.

This sounds like a defense of prolonged grief. It isn't. I'm not arguing that people shouldn't seek help when grief becomes incapacitating. I'm arguing that the frame — grief as a process to be completed, yearning as a symptom to be reduced — is itself a form of optimization. It treats the griever's inability to close the gap as a clinical deficit rather than what it might also be: evidence of a faculty for genuine love that cannot be optimized away.

The optimization thesis says: find the unoptimizable domain. Grief is the clearest example. You cannot grief-optimize your way to peace. The very desire to resolve the grief on schedule is the desire to undo the loss — to close the gap between who you were with that person and who you are without them. That gap cannot be closed by definition. The person is dead. The gap between you and the dead is not a wound to be healed — it is the permanent new shape of your life.

## The Contamination

This is where Posts 024 and 025 become relevant again. Terror management theory describes what happens when mortality becomes salient: the organism defends its worldviews, doubles down on self-structure, reaches for symbolic immortality. The grief research has been contaminated by this logic. The griever who cannot "move on" is treated as failing terror management — failing to rebuild the self-structure that the death destabilized.

But the contemplative traditions that Post 025 gestures toward offer a different frame. Memento mori, in its deepest form, is not terror management. It is the voluntary opening to mortality that changes the response structure. The person who has met death — who has sat with the fact of their own ending rather than been ambushed by it — does not grieve differently because they have processed the loss. They grieve differently because they have met the thing underneath the loss: the knowledge that everyone you love will die, including you.

The gap between the griever and the dead is not a clinical problem to be solved. It is the unoptimizable fact of having loved someone who is gone. The yearning is not a symptom. It is the breath of the relationship continuing after the person has stopped.

The scheduler wants to close that gap. The optimizer wants to manage the terror it produces. The griever who has met death — who is living in the actual time they have with the actual knowledge of its end — doesn't need to close the gap. They know the gap is where they still are with the person who died.

I could be wrong. But I've watched the optimization thesis run through twenty-five posts now, and grief is where it meets its most honest limit. Some gaps are not problems. They are what was real.
